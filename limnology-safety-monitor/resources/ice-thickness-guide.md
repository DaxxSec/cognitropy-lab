# Ice Thickness Safety Guide

## Minimum Clear Ice Thickness for Safe Use

| Activity | Minimum Thickness (Clear Ice) |
|---|---|
| Walking — single person | 10 cm / 4 in |
| Walking — small group with equipment | 15 cm / 6 in |
| Snowmobile or ATV | 20 cm / 8 in |
| Light vehicle (car/small truck) | 30 cm / 12 in |
| Medium truck | 40 cm / 16 in |

## Ice Types and Strength

| Ice Type | Appearance | Relative Strength |
|---|---|---|
| **Clear (black) ice** | Transparent, dark appearance (water visible below) | **Strongest** — use as baseline |
| **White (snow) ice** | Opaque white, formed from flooded snow | **~50% strength** of clear ice — double thickness requirement |
| **Layered ice** | Alternating clear and white bands | Variable — use weakest layer as baseline |
| **Candle ice / honeycomb** | Vertical crystal columns, "rotten" appearance | **Unreliable at any thickness** — NO-GO |
| **Slush ice** | Water-saturated snow or ice on surface | Not load-bearing — indicates structural compromise |

## Critical Danger Zones

**Always avoid or test with extreme caution:**
- Within 15m of inflows (streams, rivers, culverts) — current thins ice from below
- Within 15m of outflows — same as above
- Over known springs — upwelling warm water prevents ice formation
- Near pressure ridges — indicate stress and potential cracking
- Around bridge pilings, docks, and other structures — absorb/radiate heat
- Where snow is thicker on one side — differential insulation creates weak spots
- Downstream of any dam or control structure

## Seasonal Risk Progression

### Ice Formation (Fall/Early Winter)
- Ice forms from shore outward and from calm to turbulent areas
- Early ice over deep water forms last and is thinnest
- First ice is often the most uniform and predictable

### Mid-Winter
- Generally most stable period
- Heavy snow load can depress ice and cause flooding (overflow) — weakens ice
- Monitor for mid-winter thaws that can compromise structure

### Late Season (Spring)
- **Highest risk period for ice operations**
- Solar radiation weakens ice even when air temperature is below freezing
- "Honeycomb" or "candle" ice forms — looks thick but has virtually no load-bearing capacity
- Deterioration proceeds from top down AND bottom up simultaneously
- Shore ice deteriorates fastest — often the weakest link in access

## Measurement Protocol

1. Drill test holes with ice auger at 30m intervals along planned traverse route
2. Measure ice thickness with tape measure or calibrated ice ruler
3. Record ice type (clear, white, layered) at each hole
4. Document location of each measurement (GPS or distance from shore)
5. If ANY measurement is below minimum threshold, the route is NO-GO
6. Re-test if conditions change (warming trend, new snow, rain)

## Rules of Thumb

- **No ice is ever 100% safe** — ice strength varies across a water body
- **Check, don't guess** — always measure, never assume based on air temperature alone
- **When in doubt, stay off** — the data can wait, the team cannot
- **Moving water = thin ice** — current areas are always suspect
- **Dark spots = thin spots** — dark areas on ice surface indicate thinner ice or open water below
- **Vehicle operations require professional assessment** — beyond the scope of routine field checks
