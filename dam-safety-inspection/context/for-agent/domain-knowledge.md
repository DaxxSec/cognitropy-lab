# Domain Knowledge: Dam Safety Engineering

## Dam Classification

### By Structure Type
- **Embankment Dams**: Earth or rockfill construction. Most common type (~80% of US dams). Primary concerns: seepage, piping, internal erosion, slope instability, overtopping.
- **Concrete Gravity Dams**: Resist loads through their own weight. Concerns: uplift pressure, sliding along foundation, deterioration of concrete, drain effectiveness.
- **Concrete Arch Dams**: Transfer loads to abutments via arch action. Concerns: abutment stability, thermal stresses, joint movement, asymmetric loading.
- **Buttress Dams**: Flat slab or multiple arch supported by buttresses. Concerns: buttress stability, slab deterioration, foundation settlement.
- **RCC (Roller Compacted Concrete) Dams**: Gravity dams built with low-slump concrete placed by earthmoving equipment. Concerns: lift joint quality, permeability at lift joints, thermal cracking.

### By Hazard Potential
- **High Hazard**: Failure would likely cause loss of life
- **Significant Hazard**: Failure would cause significant economic loss but no expected loss of life
- **Low Hazard**: Failure would cause minimal economic loss and no expected loss of life

Note: Hazard potential classification is about *consequences* of failure, NOT the condition or probability of failure.

## Primary Failure Modes

### Embankment Dam Failure Modes
1. **Internal Erosion / Piping**: Progressive removal of soil particles by seepage flow through the embankment or foundation
   - Backward erosion piping: erosion initiates at downstream exit point and progresses upstream
   - Concentrated leak erosion: erosion along a crack, conduit, or other preferential flow path
   - Suffusion/suffosion: selective erosion of fine particles from the soil matrix
   - Contact erosion: erosion at the interface between coarse and fine-grained zones

2. **Slope Instability**: Shear failure of upstream or downstream slopes
   - Rapid drawdown (upstream slope)
   - Steady-state seepage (downstream slope)
   - Seismic loading (either slope)

3. **Overtopping**: Water exceeding dam crest elevation
   - Inadequate spillway capacity
   - Spillway blockage
   - Reservoir landslide-generated waves

### Concrete Dam Failure Modes
1. **Sliding**: Insufficient shear resistance along foundation contact or weak planes
2. **Overturning**: Moment from hydrostatic and uplift forces exceeding resisting moment
3. **Uplift**: Excessive water pressure at the dam-foundation interface
4. **Concrete Deterioration**: Alkali-silica reaction (ASR), freeze-thaw damage, sulfate attack
5. **Foundation Failure**: Bearing capacity, dissolution, or erosion of foundation materials

## Instrumentation Systems

### Piezometers
- Measure pore water pressure / piezometric head within embankments or foundations
- Types: standpipe (Casagrande), vibrating wire, pneumatic
- Key metric: piezometric elevation relative to reservoir level and phreatic surface
- Anomaly indicators: rising trends uncorrelated with reservoir level, sudden jumps, failure to respond to reservoir changes

### Inclinometers
- Measure lateral displacement within embankment or foundation
- Installed in vertical boreholes with guide casing
- Key metric: cumulative displacement at depth, rate of movement
- Anomaly indicators: accelerating displacement rate, displacement concentrated at a discrete zone

### Settlement Monuments / Survey Points
- Track vertical and horizontal displacement of dam crest and slopes
- Measured by precision survey (GPS, total station, or leveling)
- Key metric: settlement rate, differential settlement between adjacent points
- Anomaly indicators: accelerating settlement, differential settlement exceeding tolerances

### Seepage / Drain Flow Measurements
- Weir or flume measurements of collected seepage and drain flow
- Key metric: flow rate, turbidity/color, correlation with reservoir level
- Anomaly indicators: increasing flow uncorrelated with reservoir, turbid or discolored water (indicates soil transport), sudden changes in flow

### Strong Motion Accelerographs
- Record ground motion during seismic events
- Trigger-based recording with pre-event buffer
- Used for post-earthquake safety evaluation

## Threshold Systems

### Three-Tier Alert Framework
1. **Action Level (Green → Yellow)**: Reading exceeds normal operating range. Requires increased monitoring frequency and engineering review within a defined timeframe.
2. **Alert Level (Yellow → Orange)**: Reading indicates potentially adverse condition. Requires immediate engineering evaluation, possible emergency inspection, and notification of dam safety officials.
3. **Failure Level (Orange → Red)**: Reading indicates imminent or ongoing failure. Requires immediate activation of Emergency Action Plan.

Thresholds should be instrument-specific and account for:
- Historical baseline behavior
- Seasonal variation (temperature, rainfall, snowmelt)
- Reservoir operating range
- Physical significance of the measurement

## Inspection Types

### Regular/Routine Inspection
- Frequency: Typically annual for high-hazard dams
- Performed by dam owner's staff or consultant
- Visual inspection of all accessible features
- Review of instrumentation data trends
- Documentation of changes since last inspection

### Periodic/Comprehensive Inspection
- Frequency: Every 3-5 years for high-hazard dams
- Performed by independent qualified engineer
- Comprehensive evaluation including stability/adequacy review
- FERC Part 12D (Independent Consultant) inspection equivalent

### Special Inspection
- Triggered by: extreme events (flood, earthquake, severe weather), concerning monitoring data, or identified deficiencies
- Scope targeted to the triggering concern
- May require expedited reporting and emergency response

### Intermediate Surveillance
- Ongoing monitoring between formal inspections
- Instrumentation readings at prescribed frequency
- Visual observations during routine site visits
- Immediate reporting of unusual conditions
