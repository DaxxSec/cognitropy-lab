# High-Priority Invasive Species Reference

> Quick field reference for the most consequential invasive species in North American temperate environments.
> Always verify against your **state-specific** invasive species list — designation varies by jurisdiction.

---

## TIER 1 — FEDERAL WATCH LIST / IMMEDIATE ESCALATION

### Spotted Lanternfly (*Lycorma delicatula*)
**Type:** Insect | **Origin:** Asia | **Established Range:** PA, NJ, NY, MD, VA, DE, CT expanding rapidly
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Adults (Aug-Dec): 1 inch, gray forewings with black spots; hindwings red/black/white
- Nymphs (May-Jul): early = black with white spots; late = red with white spots
- **Egg masses:** Gray, mud-like putty patches on bark, stones, vehicles, outdoor furniture — 1 inch × 1.5 inch
- Prefers **tree of heaven** (*Ailanthus altissima*) as host — their presence together is a strong signal
**What to do:** Scrape and destroy egg masses (bag them, sealable). Photograph and report to your state ag dept immediately. Do NOT move firewood, vehicles, or equipment out of known infested zones.

---

### Giant Hogweed (*Heracleum mantegazzianum*)
**Type:** Plant (Apiaceae family) | **Origin:** Central Asia
**⚠️ EXTREME HAZARD: Do NOT touch, brush, or contact in any way**
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Massive size: 8-14 feet tall
- Stems: hollow, ridged, with purple blotches and coarse white hairs
- Leaves: deeply incised, 5 feet wide
- Flowers: white, flat-topped umbels up to 2.5 feet across (blooms June-July)
- **Compare to native cow parsnip** (*H. maximum*): hogweed is much larger; cow parsnip has smooth (not purple-blotched) stems
**What to do:** Do NOT touch. Photograph at distance. Report to state DEC/ag department immediately. Sap + sunlight = severe chemical burns, permanent scarring, potential blindness.

---

### Emerald Ash Borer (*Agrilus planipennis*)
**Type:** Insect | **Origin:** Asia | **Established Range:** Most of eastern US and expanding
**Diagnostic Features:**
- D-shaped exit holes in ash bark (the "D" shape is definitive)
- S-shaped larval galleries under bark (visible when bark peeled)
- Adults: emerald green, 0.5 inch beetles (rarely seen)
- Epicormic sprouting from base and trunk (tree trying to survive)
- Crown dieback from top down
**Host trees:** All ash species (*Fraxinus*) — white, green, black, blue ash
**What to do:** Do not move ash firewood. Report new county-level detections to state forestry.

## TIER 2 — ESTABLISHED BUT HIGH-IMPACT, ACTIVE MANAGEMENT RECOMMENDED

### Japanese Knotweed (*Reynoutria japonica*)
**Type:** Plant | **Origin:** East Asia
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Hollow, bamboo-like stems with raised nodes (but NOT bamboo)
- Leaves: broadly heart-shaped at base, flat bottom edge, alternate
- Stems: reddish-brown in winter; bright green to red-pink in spring
- **Grows in massive stands**, especially on stream banks
- Spring shoots edible (like rhubarb) — but knowing this, many people transplant it accidentally
**Why it matters:** Rhizomes can penetrate concrete. Root systems 9 feet deep, spread 65 feet horizontal. One fragment = new colony.
**Control:** Long-term commitment — repeated cutting, herbicide injection into cut stems.

---

### Kudzu (*Pueraria montana*)
**Type:** Vine | **Origin:** East Asia | **Established Range:** SE US, expanding northward
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Enormous compound leaves (3 leaflets, each up to 4 inches across)
- Covers entire trees, utility poles, structures in continuous mats
- Purple, grape-scented flowers (summer)
- Can grow 1 foot per day in peak season
**Why it matters:** "The vine that ate the South." Smothers all vegetation beneath it.

---

### Purple Loosestrife (*Lythrum salicaria*)
**Type:** Plant | **Origin:** Europe/Asia
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Wetland/riparian environments
- Tall (2-5 feet), upright, with magenta/purple flower spikes (July-Aug)
- Leaves: lance-shaped, opposite or whorled, slightly clasping stem
- Stem: distinctly 4-6 sided (ridged)
- Produces ~2.7 million seeds per plant per year
**Why it matters:** Forms impenetrable monocultures in wetlands, eliminating waterfowl nesting habitat.

---

### Garlic Mustard (*Alliaria petiolata*)
**Type:** Plant | **Origin:** Europe
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Early spring (March-April): triangular-toothed heart-shaped basal rosettes
- Spring bolt: 1-4 feet tall, small white 4-petaled flowers
- **Distinctive garlic odor when leaves crushed** — definitive
- Long slender seedpods (siliques) in summer
**Why it matters:** Allelopathic — kills mycorrhizal fungi that native trees depend on. Effectively sterilizes the forest floor for native wildflowers. One of the most ecologically damaging invasives in eastern US forests.

---

### Autumn/Olive/Russian Olive (*Elaeagnus* spp.)
**Type:** Shrub/small tree | **Origin:** Asia/Europe
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Silver-green leaves with silver scales on undersides (distinctive when backlit)
- Small fragrant yellow flowers (spring)
- Red/silver berries in fall (birds eat and spread widely)
- Nitrogen-fixing — alters soil chemistry
**Why it matters:** Rapidly spreads along roadsides, forest edges, riparian corridors. Fixes nitrogen, changing soil chemistry to favor itself over natives.

---

### Barberry (*Berberis thunbergii* — Japanese; *B. vulgaris* — common)
**Type:** Shrub | **Origin:** Japan/Europe
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Dense, thorny shrub (thorns single on Japanese; triple on common barberry)
- Small rounded leaves, red in fall
- Yellow flowers in spring; red/coral berries persist through winter
- Dark yellow inner wood when stems cut (diagnostic)
**Why it matters:** Proven to increase white-footed mouse density → increased Lyme disease risk. Alters forest understory light dynamics.

---

### Tree of Heaven (*Ailanthus altissima*)
**Type:** Tree | **Origin:** China
**Diagnostic Features:**
- Compound leaves, 11-41 leaflets per leaf
- **Rank, burnt peanut butter odor** when leaves or bark broken (definitive)
- Smooth gray bark, resembles sumac from distance
- Winged seeds (samaras) in large clusters
- Can regenerate from root sprouts aggressively after cutting
**Why it matters:** Primary host for spotted lanternfly (see Tier 1). Fast-growing, allelopathic. Classic disturbance-following pioneer.

## TIER 3 — MONITOR AND DOCUMENT

### Norway Maple (*Acer platanoides*)
**Diagnostic Features:** Milky sap from broken leaf stem (native maples = clear sap). Dense shade canopy suppresses native understory.

### Multiflora Rose (*Rosa multiflora*)
**Diagnostic Features:** Stipules (where leaf meets stem) with fringed/comb-like projections — distinctive. Small white 5-petaled flowers in clusters. Dense arching canes.

### Oriental Bittersweet (*Celastrus orbiculatus*)
**Diagnostic Features:** Berries/capsules along the ENTIRE length of stem (native American bittersweet = berries only at branch tips). Girdling vines kill host trees.

### Common Reed / Phragmites (*Phragmites australis* ssp. *australis*)
**Diagnostic Features:** Non-native strain: dense monocultures in wetlands; stems with visible nodes remaining gray through winter; leaves with ligule > 1mm. Native strain present but declining.

## QUICK REFERENCE: Spring Anomaly Signals

Early spring is the best time for invasive detection:

| If you see... | Suspect... |
|---|---|
| Green shrubs leafing when everything else is still dormant | Autumn olive, Japanese barberry, privet, burning bush |
| Dense mats of low green rosettes on forest floor before natives emerge | Garlic mustard, lesser celandine, Japanese pachysandra |
| Hollow bamboo-like reddish shoots emerging from stream banks | Japanese knotweed |
| Muddy patches on bark that don't belong | Spotted lanternfly egg masses |
| D-shaped holes in ash bark | Emerald ash borer |
| Any plant with "feels wrong for the season" phenology | Photograph, ID, log — anomaly detected |
