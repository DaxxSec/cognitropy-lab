# Underwater Archaeology Excavation — Core Concepts

Background the agent reads before acting. Optimised for fast recall, not exhaustive theory. Pairs maritime-archaeology fundamentals with the cost-benefit framing this workspace applies to them.

## Site Formation Theory

A submerged archaeological site is the *end state* of processes that began at deposition, not a snapshot of the original event. Keith Muckelroy's foundational model (*Maritime Archaeology*, 1978) frames a wreck as a sequence of **extracting filters** and **scrambling devices**:

- **Process of wrecking** — the original loss event; determines initial distribution.
- **Salvage** — contemporary and later removal of valuable/accessible material (an extracting filter).
- **Disintegration of perishables** — organics, fastenings, and structure decay differentially.
- **Seabed movement** — currents, scour, and sediment transport rearrange and bury material (scrambling).

What survives is biased toward the durable and the deeply-buried. **Anaerobic, low-energy, rapidly-burying environments** (fine marine mud, anoxic Baltic-type water) preserve organics extraordinarily well; **high-energy, oxygenated, biologically-active** environments (warm shallow reef, *Teredo navalis* shipworm range) destroy wood within years. Preservation potential is a primary driver of both significance (benefit) and conservation cost.

## The In-Situ Preservation Doctrine

The central decision of the discipline is **in situ preservation vs. excavation**, and the modern default is preservation in place:

- **UNESCO 2001 Annex Rule 1:** "The protection of underwater cultural heritage through in situ preservation shall be considered as the first option." Excavation is a justified exception, not the goal.
- In-situ preservation is **not "do nothing."** It is active management: monitoring, sometimes physical stabilisation (sandbagging, sediment recharge, debris/anode protection, reburial). It carries real, recurring cost.
- Excavation is **unrepeatable** — it destroys the stratigraphic context it records. Justification therefore rests on a research question that can only be answered by excavation, or on a threat that will destroy the resource anyway (rescue/salvage archaeology).

In CBA terms: in-situ is a low-capital, recurring-cost option whose benefit is *option value* (the site, and future technique, remain available); excavation is a high-capital, irreversible option whose benefit is realised knowledge and recovered material, set against the lifetime conservation/curation liability it creates.

## How Cost-Benefit Analysis Maps onto Excavation

CBA here is **cost-effectiveness / multi-criteria**, not pure monetisation — heritage benefits are non-market goods. The ledger:

**Cost side (lifecycle, not just fieldwork):**
- **Survey & evaluation** — geophysics, diver/ROV evaluation, trial trenching.
- **Excavation fieldwork** — vessel day-rate, diver-days (rationed by decompression), equipment, recording.
- **Conservation** — frequently the *largest* single cost; waterlogged organics and marine iron require multi-year treatment. The field-wide "conservation backlog" is the symptom of ignoring this.
- **Curation in perpetuity** — climate-controlled storage, documentation, and access *forever*. Discounted to present value it is a large liability.
- **Post-excavation analysis & publication** — specialist analysis, dating, archiving.

**Benefit side (valuation methods):**
- **Significance** — research, historic, aesthetic/artistic, communal value (see rubric below) used as a benefit proxy or weight.
- **Threat-discounted loss** — the expected loss of heritage value if the site is *not* excavated, used to value rescue.
- **Contingent / public value** — willingness-to-pay, visitor/exhibition value, education.

**Decision rules:** net benefit / B:C ratio where monetisation is defensible; **multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA)** with weighted significance and cost criteria otherwise; **expected value under threat** for rescue cases. Always discount future costs and benefits, and run sensitivity analysis.

## Survey & Detection Methods

Remote sensing precedes any disturbance — it is cheap relative to diving and scopes the problem:

- **Side-scan sonar** — acoustic imagery of seabed relief; finds exposed structure/mounds; high coverage, moderate resolution.
- **Multibeam echosounder (MBES)** — bathymetry and 3-D relief; quantifies scour and exposure.
- **Marine magnetometer / gradiometer** — detects ferrous mass (iron fastenings, cannon, anchors, engines); finds buried iron-bearing sites; no imagery.
- **Sub-bottom profiler (chirp/boomer)** — penetrates sediment to image *buried* structure and stratigraphy.
- **Diver / ROV / AUV visual + photogrammetry** — ground-truth and high-resolution recording.

## Recording Methods

Because excavation destroys context, recording *is* the product:

- **Photogrammetry / Structure-from-Motion (SfM)** — now the dominant 3-D recording method; overlapping photos → georeferenced point cloud and orthophoto. Cheap, fast, accurate.
- **Direct Survey Method (DSM)** — distance measurements between fixed datums, least-squares adjusted (e.g. Site Recorder software). Pre-photogrammetry workhorse, still used for control.
- **Planning frames / grids** — physical reference for hand-drawing in poor visibility.
- **Context/stratigraphic recording** — single-context planning adapted to the seabed; the unit of excavation and the unit of cost.

## Excavation & Sediment-Removal Methods

- **Airlift** — compressed air in a vertical pipe lifts sediment by suction; good for fine overburden, gentle if controlled.
- **Water dredge (induction dredge)** — a water jet induces flow through a pipe; works at any depth/orientation, no compressor; standard tool.
- **Hand-fanning** — finest control near fragile finds; slowest.
- **Grid + controlled spit excavation** — systematic removal in measured units to preserve stratigraphy.
- **Dredging spoil control** — spoil must be screened (finds recovery) and deposited away from the site.

Method choice trades **cost/speed** against **site disturbance** and **recovery quality** — a direct CBA sub-problem.

## Conservation Realities (the dominant cost driver)

- **Waterlogged wood** — cell walls are degraded; drying without treatment causes catastrophic shrinkage/collapse. Treatment: **PEG (polyethylene glycol)** bulking, often two molecular weights, then controlled drying or **freeze-drying**. Large timbers take *years* (the *Mary Rose* hull: ~decades; the *Vasa*: ongoing post-treatment management).
- **Marine iron** — forms **concretion** (corrosion + carbonate); the original metal may be a void. Requires chloride extraction (alkaline soaking / electrolytic reduction) over many months to years, or it spalls apart in store.
- **Organics (leather, rope, textile, bone)** — analogous waterlogged treatments; never air-dry.
- **Composite and copper-alloy** objects each have distinct protocols.
- **Rule of thumb:** budget conservation + curation as a *multiple* of recovery cost, not a rounding error.

## Diving & Access Constraints

- Bottom time is governed by **decompression** — no-decompression limits (NDL) shrink fast with depth; recreational/scientific air diving is practically limited to ~30–40 m.
- Deeper work needs **surface-supplied** diving, **mixed gas (trimix)**, or **saturation** diving — each step is a large cost multiplier.
- Beyond ~100–150 m, human diving is replaced by **ROV/AUV**; deep sites are technology-bound, shifting the cost structure from diver-days to vehicle/vessel days.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Costing the boat, not the lifecycle** — ignoring conservation/curation; the classic project-killer.
- **Excavating by default** — treating in-situ preservation as the fallback rather than the first option.
- **Significance inflation** — every site feels significant to its excavator; an unstructured benefit score is not defensible.
- **Recovering the photogenic, not the informative** — letting display appeal override research value-per-cost.
- **Single-point CBA** — a recommendation with no sensitivity analysis collapses the first time a funder questions the discount rate.
- **Conflating salvage with archaeology** — commercial recovery destroys the context that gives objects research value.

## Operating Constraints

- **UNESCO 2001 Convention** and its Annex Rules govern method, ethics, and the in-situ default; commercial exploitation is prohibited.
- **Jurisdiction** is layered (territorial sea / contiguous zone / EEZ / the Area) with different obligations; sunken state vessels retain **sovereign immunity** regardless of age.
- **Human remains and war graves** carry obligations independent of archaeological value.
- Excavation is **irreversible**; recording quality is the only safeguard against the destruction it causes.
