# Falconry Bird Training — Working Concepts

Background the agent reads before planning training, interpreting a weight log, or drafting a stakeholder communication. Optimised for fast recall, not exhaustive theory. U.S.-centric on the regulatory side; the training and husbandry substance is general.

## What falconry is (and the legal frame)

Falconry is taking wild quarry in its natural state with a trained raptor. In the U.S. it is legal but tightly licensed: a falconer holds both the bird and a permit, and almost everything about acquiring, housing, transferring, and losing a raptor is a reportable event. The federal baseline is the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA)** plus **50 CFR 21.82** (falconry standards and permitting — moved there from §21.29 in the 2022 reorganization of 50 CFR Part 21). Falconry is **state-administered**: USFWS certifies a state's program, then the state issues permits under its own regulations, which are frequently stricter than the federal floor. There is no separate federal falconry permit for residents of certified states.

## The stakeholder map (the organizing spine)

Every falconry program is a hub of relationships, each with its own document type, cadence, and deadline. This is what "stakeholder communication templates" organizes:

| Stakeholder | What they need from you | Trigger / cadence |
|-------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **State wildlife agency** (falconry coordinator) | Permit application/renewal, facility inspection, take reports, acquisition/disposition | Annual renewal; per-event reporting |
| **U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service** | Banding/marking compliance, Form 3-186A data (often via state e-systems) | On acquisition/transfer/loss, typically within 10 days |
| **Sponsor** (Master/General falconer) | Progress reports, training questions, oversight for apprentices | Agreed cadence (monthly/quarterly) over the 2-year apprenticeship |
| **Apprentice** (if you sponsor one) | Guidance, sign-offs, facility/bird review | Reciprocal of the above |
| **Avian veterinarian** | Clear intake history for any health concern | On any illness/injury, fast |
| **Landowners** | Access request, liability/courtesy, stewardship | Per-season; renew yearly |
| **Abatement clients** | Proposals, scopes of work, service reports | Contract lifecycle; per-shift reporting |
| **Breeders / sellers** | Acquisition paperwork, transfer reports | On purchase of a captive-bred bird |
| **Clubs / orgs** (NAFA, state club) | Membership, meet participation, conservation | Ongoing |
| **The public / education audiences** | Accurate, welfare-forward messaging | As needed |

## Permit classes (U.S.)

- **Apprentice** — entry class. Requires a **sponsor** (a General or Master falconer, usually with ≥2 years at General or above). Minimum age commonly 12–14 depending on state (parental consent for minors). May possess **one** raptor of a permitted species. Apprenticeship lasts a minimum of **2 years**.
- **General** — after ≥2 years as an Apprentice. May possess up to **three** raptors. Broader species access.
- **Master** — after ≥5 years as a General. May possess up to **five** wild-taken raptors (and, under many interpretations, additional captive-bred birds). Eligible to sponsor apprentices and to fly certain restricted species (e.g. some eagles, with additional eagle permits).

State rules layer on top: possession counts, allowed species, take seasons, and facility standards all vary. Always confirm with the state coordinator.

## Species selection

- **Red-tailed hawk** (*Buteo jamaicensis*) — the classic North American apprentice bird; hardy, forgiving, excellent on rabbit and squirrel. **Passage** (first-year wild) birds are preferred for apprentices.
- **American kestrel** (*Falco sparverius*) — small falcon, also apprentice-legal in many states; less margin for weight error (low body mass) so demands precise scale work.
- **Harris's hawk** (*Parabuteo unicinctus*) — social, highly trainable, popular for general/master falconers; usually captive-bred; the only hawk that hunts cooperatively.
- **Peregrine falcon, gyrfalcon, prairie falcon, merlin** — longwings for waiting-on/game-hawking; advanced, telemetry-mandatory.
- **Goshawk, Cooper's hawk** — accipiters; demanding, high-strung, expert birds.
- **Sourcing:** **passage** (trapped first-year wild), **eyass** (taken from the nest), **imprint** (hand-raised, behaviorally bonded — powerful but prone to screaming/aggression if done wrong), or **captive-bred** from a permitted breeder.

## Weight management and condition

The central training lever — and the easiest thing to get dangerously wrong.

- **Flying weight** — the body mass at which a given bird is keen to work for food but not weak. It is individual, seasonal, and discovered empirically, not read from a chart.
- **Yarak** — a hawk in peak hunting condition: alert, eager, "looking for it." Used mostly of shortwings/accipiters.
- **Sharp-set** — keen with appetite; ready to fly to the fist or lure for food.
- **Condition vs. fat** — a fat bird ("high") ignores the falconer; an over-reduced bird is at medical risk. The art is finding the narrow responsive band.
- **Going light** — unplanned weight loss below the safe band; a red flag for illness (frounce, aspergillosis, parasites), **not** a training state to chase. This is a vet event.
- **Body condition scoring** — palpate the keel (breast muscle): a sharp, prominent keel signals low condition; a well-muscled keel with appropriate weight signals health.

## Manning and the human-bird relationship

- **Manning** — habituating the bird to the falconer, people, dogs, vehicles, and the field. Begins with the bird sitting hooded/calm on the fist and eating in company.
- **Hooding** — a well-fitted hood calms the bird by removing visual stimulus; essential for transport and stressful settings.
- **Imprinting** — a bird raised by humans during its critical period bonds to people; produces tameness but risks screaming and food-aggression if mismanaged.

## The training progression

1. **Manning & fist-feeding** — bird steps up, eats calmly on the glove.
2. **Jump-to-fist / step-up** — short hops to the fist for food.
3. **Creance flights** — flights on a long light line (the *creance*) at increasing distance; confirms recall before risking free flight.
4. **Free flight** — off the creance; the bird flies to the fist/lure reliably at distance.
5. **Entering / making to quarry** — the bird is introduced to legal quarry (rabbit, squirrel, game birds) and learns to hunt in partnership; for longwings, learning to "wait on" overhead.
6. **Conditioning & fitness** — building the muscle and wind to hunt effectively.

## Equipment (the furniture)

- **Anklets & jesses** — leather straps on the legs. **Aylmeri** anklets take removable grommeted jesses; **field jesses** are slit-less to avoid snagging on cover, **mews jesses** have a slit for the swivel.
- **Swivel & leash** — connect jesses to the perch/tether safely.
- **Hood** — calms the bird; Anglo-Indian and Dutch patterns common.
- **Bells & telemetry** — bells for audible location; a radio **transmitter** (tail-mount or leg-mount) is effectively mandatory for free-flying birds. Vendors: Marshall, Communications Specialists, Tracking Solutions.
- **Perches** — **bow perch** for hawks, **block perch** for falcons; padded for foot health.
- **Mews & weathering area** — the indoor housing (mews) and outdoor sheltered area (weathering yard); subject to facility-inspection standards (size, safety, predator-proofing, double-door or safety entry).
- **Scale, bath pan, lure, glove, hawking bag, creance.**

## Husbandry and health (know the names; route to a vet)

- **Mutes** — droppings; appearance (color, consistency, urates) is a health signal.
- **Castings / pellets** — indigestible material (fur, feathers, bone) regurgitated ~daily; their timing and content track digestion.
- **Common ailments:**
  - **Frounce** (trichomoniasis, *Trichomonas gallinae*) — cheesy lesions in the mouth/crop, from feeding infected pigeons/doves; treatable, can be fatal.
  - **Aspergillosis** — fungal respiratory disease from spores in damp/dirty housing or stress; insidious, serious.
  - **Bumblefoot** (pododermatitis) — foot infection/swelling from poor perching, trauma, or obesity.
  - **Coccidiosis, sour crop, sour crop/slow crop, *Capillaria* and other parasites.**
  - **Going light** — see weight section; treat as medical until proven otherwise.
- **Coping** — trimming/reshaping overgrown beak and talons.

## Molt

The annual feather replacement, usually over the warmer months when many birds are "put up" (rested, fed up, not flown). Falconers track which feathers drop and regrow; **imping** is the repair of a broken feather by splicing in a matching molted feather. Molt timing and feather records belong in the husbandry log and can matter for inspection and identification.

## Common failure modes

- **Chasing weight instead of reading condition** — driving a bird light to force response, masking illness, risking the bird.
- **Free-flying without telemetry** — a recoverable loss becomes a permanent one.
- **Missed regulatory deadlines** — lapsed renewal, late 3-186A acquisition/disposition report, unreported mortality.
- **Facility not inspection-ready** — undersized mews, unsafe perching, no safety entry; delays the first bird.
- **Verbal-only landowner agreements** — no record of permission, quarry, or dates when a dispute arises.
- **Undocumented training** — no weight log means no sponsor report, no defensible welfare record, no audit trail.

## Operating constraints

- Federal: MBTA + 50 CFR 21.82; state: each state's falconry regulations (stricter floor varies).
- Possession limits and allowed species are set by permit class and state.
- Acquisition, disposition, transfer, banding/marking, and loss/mortality are reportable; reporting windows (commonly 10 days for 3-186A events) are hard deadlines.
- Eagles, and movement of birds across state/international lines, carry additional permit and CITES obligations.
- The agent advises on structure and communications; it does not render legal or veterinary judgment.
