# Concepts — EP Threat Assessment × Apprenticeship Progression

This workspace fuses two bodies of knowledge: **executive-protection threat assessment** (the operational domain) and **competency-based apprenticeship tracking** (the development technique). The unifying idea is that a threat-assessment artifact is simultaneously an operational product and a *workplace-based assessment* of the agent who produced it.

---

## Part 1 — Executive Protection Threat Assessment

### 1.1 The protective triad and concentric rings

Protection is **defence in depth** organised as concentric rings:

- **Inner ring** — the close-protection officers (the "body"): cover and evacuate the principal.
- **Middle ring** — the security perimeter: access control, magnetometers, posts, residence/venue hardening.
- **Outer ring** — advance work and **protective intelligence**: the ring that does threat assessment. It is the only ring that can *prevent* an attack rather than survive one.

A program is only as strong as its outer ring. Most preventable failures are intelligence and advance failures, not failures of close-protection reaction.

### 1.2 Risk decomposition

Two complementary formulations, both used here:

- **Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Consequence.** Threat is about the adversary; vulnerability is about us; consequence is about the principal's value-at-risk. You can lower risk by attacking any factor — usually vulnerability is the cheapest lever.
- **Threat = Intent × Capability**, gated by **Opportunity**. An actor with intent but no capability is a *howler*; capability but no intent is irrelevant until intent forms; both plus opportunity is an *imminent* threat. (See *hunters vs howlers*, §1.6.)

### 1.3 The hostile planning cycle (attack cycle)

Hostile actors who plan violence move through an observable cycle. Each phase is an **interdiction window** — a place a protective program can detect and disrupt:

1. **Target selection** — adversary chooses the principal (grievance, ideology, notoriety, opportunity).
2. **Initial surveillance** — gathering pattern-of-life, routines, security posture.
3. **Final target selection** — committing to this principal / this event.
4. **Pre-attack surveillance & planning** — detailed reconnaissance, choosing method and location.
5. **Rehearsal / dry run** — testing the plan, probing response.
6. **Execution** — the attack.
7. **Escape / exploitation** — getaway, or martyrdom/claim.

The cheapest interdictions are early (phases 2–5) and rely on **surveillance detection** and **protective intelligence**, not on out-shooting an attacker at phase 6.

### 1.4 Path to intended violence (Calhoun & Weston)

A complementary behavioural model focused on the *individual's* trajectory, used in `/adversary-assessment` and `/attack-cycle-map`:

**Grievance → Ideation → Research & planning → Preparation → Breach → Attack.**

Movement *along* this path (not static traits) is the signal. The U.S. Secret Service Exceptional Case Study Project (ECSP) found attacks are the end of an "understandable and often discernible process of thinking and behaviour" — and that **there is no useful demographic profile**.

### 1.5 Surveillance detection

- **Surveillance detection (SD)** — passive/observational: are *we* being watched? Run a **surveillance detection route (SDR)** that forces a follower to reveal themselves through time, distance, and unnatural behaviour.
- **Counter-surveillance (CS)** — active: a separate element watches the watchers.
- **TEDD** heuristic — be suspicious of the same person/vehicle seen across **T**ime, **E**nvironment, **D**istance, and exhibiting poor **D**emeanour.
- **Pre-attack / pre-incident indicators (PINs)** — observed reconnaissance, photography of security measures, testing of access, probing questions, "casing" behaviour.

### 1.6 Behavioural Threat Assessment & Management (BTAM)

Structured professional judgement, not checklists-as-scores:

- **Hunters vs Howlers** (Calhoun & Weston) — *hunters* engage in attack-related behaviours and rarely threaten; *howlers* make threats but rarely act. Communicated threats and actual risk are weakly correlated.
- **TRAP-18** (Meloy) — 8 **proximal warning behaviours** (pathway, fixation, identification, novel aggression, energy burst, leakage, last resort, directly communicated threat) + 10 **distal characteristics**. Used as a structured lens in `/adversary-assessment`.
- **WAVR-21** — workplace/targeted-violence risk; useful for insider and grievance cases.
- **Leakage** — communication to a third party of intent to do harm; one of the most actionable indicators.

### 1.7 Advance work

"Time spent in advance is never wasted." A protective **advance** reconnoitres a site/route before the principal arrives. Core deliverables: arrival/departure points (APOD), primary + alternate + emergency routes, **safe haven / hold room**, nearest **Level I/II trauma center** with time-distance, command post, access control plan, emergency egress, and a site-specific **emergency action plan (EAP)**. **CPTED** (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design) frames the physical survey. **CARVER** (Criticality, Accessibility, Recuperability, Vulnerability, Effect, Recognizability) frames "what would an adversary target here."

### 1.8 Movement & immediate-action drills

- **Formations** — diamond/box around the principal; spacing scales with threat and crowd.
- **Motorcade** — lead, principal (limo), follow/CAT (counter-assault team); embus/debus is the highest-risk moment.
- **AOP / AOV** — Attack-on-Principal / Attack-on-Vehicle: the drill is **sound off → cover → evacuate**. EP doctrine is *get off the X* (leave the kill zone), not stand and fight.

### 1.9 Common failure modes

- Treating *communicated threats* as the main signal (ignoring quiet hunters).
- Static profiling instead of behavioural-trajectory assessment.
- Advance work that lists assets but never derives **decisions** (routes, drills, triggers).
- Risk tiers with no calibration — everything is "high."
- Single route, no alternates; no medical plan; no rehearsed AOP.

## Part 2 — Apprenticeship Progression Tracking

### 2.1 Why a competency model

EP is a craft acquired by graduated real-world practice. Tenure ≠ competence. A defensible program tracks *what an agent can be trusted to do unsupervised*, on evidence.

### 2.2 The Dreyfus skill ladder mapped to EP roles

| Dreyfus stage | EP role | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Novice | **Protective Agent Trainee** | Rule-following; needs the recipe; little situational judgement |
| Advanced beginner | **Shift / Post Agent** | Handles routine posts; recognises recurring situations |
| Competent | **Advance Agent** | Plans deliberately; prioritises; owns an advance |
| Proficient | **Detail Leader** | Reads situations holistically; anticipates; leads a detail |
| Expert | **Detail Commander / Program Lead** | Intuitive grasp; designs programs; supervises and teaches |

### 2.3 Entrustable Professional Activities (EPAs)

An **EPA** is a unit of professional work you can entrust to someone once they're ready. This workspace defines eight, each mapped to a command:

| EPA | Activity | Command |
|---|---|---|
| EPA-1 | Conduct a venue/residence/route advance survey | `/advance-survey` |
| EPA-2 | Build a protectee risk profile | `/protectee-risk-profile` |
| EPA-3 | Assess a threat actor (capability/intent/opportunity) | `/adversary-assessment` |
| EPA-4 | Plan and analyse a movement route | `/route-threat-analysis` |
| EPA-5 | Design and run a surveillance-detection plan | `/surveillance-detection-plan` |
| EPA-6 | Map a threat to the attack cycle and recommend interdiction | `/attack-cycle-map` |
| EPA-7 | Build and rehearse an emergency action plan | `/eap-builder` |
| EPA-8 | Maintain protective-intelligence watch / triage threat comms | `/adversary-assessment` + `/attack-cycle-map` |

### 2.4 The entrustment scale (Chen / ten Cate, adapted)

Every EPA is rated per apprentice on a five-level scale:

1. **Observe only** — not permitted to perform; watches a qualified agent.
2. **Direct (proactive) supervision** — performs with supervisor present in the room/on the X.
3. **Indirect (reactive) supervision** — performs alone; supervisor reachable and reviews after.
4. **Unsupervised** — fully entrusted for live operations.
5. **Supervise/teach others** — can train and sign off juniors on this EPA.

A level is granted only on accumulated **workplace-based-assessment (WBA)** evidence — never on time served alone.

### 2.5 Assessment levels — Miller's pyramid

WBAs should probe the right level of Miller's pyramid:

- **Knows** (facts) → quiz / `context` recall.
- **Knows how** (applied reasoning) → plan critique.
- **Shows how** (demonstration) → graded artifact in a scenario.
- **Does** (performance in practice) → graded artifact on a *live* operation under supervision — the gold standard, and what `/competency-signoff` targets.

### 2.6 Cognitive apprenticeship & deliberate practice

- **Cognitive apprenticeship** (Collins, Brown & Newman): **modelling → coaching → scaffolding → articulation → reflection → exploration.** `/apprentice-tasking` removes scaffolding deliberately as entrustment grows.
- **Deliberate practice** (Ericsson): targeted reps at the edge of ability, with immediate specific feedback, on the *weakest* competency — not generic repetition. `/progression-review` finds the weakest EPA; `/apprentice-tasking` assigns practice against it.
- **After-action review (AAR)** is the reflection engine: what was supposed to happen, what happened, why the delta, what we sustain/improve — yielding both operational lessons and individual competency observations.

### 2.7 Failure modes (development side)

- Advancing on tenure/likeability instead of evidence (the cardinal sin — it endangers the principal).
- "Halo" grading: one good advance inflating every EPA.
- Practising strengths instead of the weakest competency.
- No entrustment ceiling: letting an under-entrusted agent run a live high-exposure EPA.
- Evidence that isn't reproducible — a grade a second assessor couldn't defend.

## Part 3 — The fusion in one sentence

A threat assessment that doesn't make the principal safer is a bad operational product; a threat assessment that doesn't tell you what its author can now be trusted to do alone is a wasted training opportunity — this workspace insists every artifact does both.
