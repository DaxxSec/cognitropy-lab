# Core Workflows — Step-by-Step Procedures

## Workflow 1: MCI Response & Triage Execution

**Objective:** Rapidly assess and categorize patients during a mass casualty incident, allocate resources appropriately, and document decisions for quality improvement.

**Time Frame:** Minutes to hours depending on MCI scale  
**Participants:** Incident commander, triage officers, medical team, transport coordinators  
**Outcome:** Categorized patient roster, resource utilization report, lessons learned

### Detailed Steps

#### STEP 1: Scene Safety & Incident Activation (0–5 minutes)
1. **Incident commander** declares Mass Casualty Incident
2. Activate emergency operations center (EOC) and hospital incident command system (HICS)
3. Request mutual aid / additional resources (EMS, hospitals, state/federal if needed)
4. Establish incident objectives and resource allocation authority
5. Brief triage team on: MCI type (vehicle crash, building collapse, etc.), estimated patient count, available resources
6. Identify staging areas: Command post, triage area, treatment zones (Immediate/Delayed/Minor), transport staging

#### STEP 2: Initial Triage Area Setup (5–10 minutes)
1. Identify triage site (ideally upwind, away from hazard, accessible to transport)
2. Position triage officers at entry to patient flow
3. Establish documentation system (clipboard, forms, or electronic if available)
4. Set up supplies: Bandages, tourniquets, airway adjuncts, rapid assessment tools
5. Brief triage officers: Protocol to use (START, SALT, facility protocol), triage categorization, any special considerations

#### STEP 3: Rapid Triage Assessment (Ongoing, each patient ~60 seconds)
1. **First patient approach:** Greet, assess responsiveness
2. **Apply triage protocol** (START, JumpSTART, or facility standard):
   - Assess respiration, perfusion, mental status per protocol
   - Assign triage category (Immediate/Delayed/Minor/Expectant)
   - If pediatric (ages 1–8): Use JumpSTART modifications
3. **Document decision:** Mark patient with triage tag (color code), record on roster
4. **Special interventions** if time permits (SALT protocol):
   - Airway positioning, hemorrhage control, splinting
5. **Route patient** to appropriate zone (treatment or transport staging)

#### STEP 4: Resource Allocation & Monitoring (Ongoing)
1. **Track resource consumption:**
   - Count patients by triage category
   - Monitor bed capacity, staff allocation
   - Identify bottlenecks (e.g., high percentage of Immediate patients overwhelming surge capacity)
2. **Escalate surge protocol** if needed:
   - > 40% Immediate patients → Activate Phase 2 surge (increase staffing by 40%)
   - > 50% Immediate patients → Activate Phase 3 (divert routine admits, activate mutual aid)
3. **Coordinate transport:**
   - Match patient acuity to receiving hospital capacity
   - Balance load across receiving facilities
4. **Request additional resources** if forecast exceeds available capacity

#### STEP 5: Ongoing Triage (Until Scene Cleared)
1. Continue triage of arriving patients
2. Re-assess patients in holding areas if status changes
3. Update incident commander on patient count, resource utilization, anticipated additional capacity needs
4. Communicate with transport coordinators on current and expected load

#### STEP 6: Scene Closure & Transition (When patient flow ends)
1. Conduct final triage area sweep to ensure no patients missed
2. Release triage area and demobilize teams
3. Transition to post-incident phase (evacuation, decontamination if needed)
4. Ensure all documentation is complete and compiled

#### STEP 7: After-Action Review (Within 48 hours)
1. Compile incident documentation: Patient roster, triage decisions, resource utilization, timeline
2. Conduct structured after-action review (AAR):
   - What went well? (e.g., rapid triage, effective resource allocation)
   - What could be improved? (e.g., communication delays, documentation gaps)
   - Root cause analysis for any errors or near-misses
   - Action items with assigned owners and target dates
3. Generate formal incident report (see `/incident-report` command)
4. Identify training or protocol changes needed
5. Share findings with staff and leadership

## Workflow 2: ED Surge Management

**Objective:** Monitor ED census, activate surge protocols at appropriate thresholds, manage patient flow during high-volume periods, and safely de-escalate when demand decreases.

**Time Frame:** Hours to days  
**Participants:** ED director, charge nurse, triage nurse, operations, bed management  
**Outcome:** Maintained ED capacity, appropriate ESI triage, patient safety, resource efficiency

### Detailed Steps

#### STEP 1: Baseline Monitoring & Threshold Setting (Routine, Daily)
1. **Establish capacity baseline:**
   - Define normal ED census (e.g., 80 patients/day)
   - Define surge thresholds:
     - Green (Routine): 0–80% capacity (0–64 patients)
     - Yellow (Activate Phase 1): 80–100% capacity (64–80 patients)
     - Orange (Activate Phase 2): 100–120% capacity (80–96 patients)
     - Red (Activate Phase 3): 120%+ capacity (96+ patients)
2. **Monitor in real-time:**
   - Track current census every 30 minutes
   - Note arrival rate trends (increasing? decreasing?)
   - Identify acuity mix (% ESI-1, ESI-2, etc.)
3. **Forecast next 12 hours** using historical patterns and current trends

#### STEP 2: Early Alert & Preparation (When approaching Yellow threshold, ~80% capacity)
1. **ED director notifies** relevant staff:
   - Nursing manager: Prepare flexible staffing schedule
   - Ancillary services (lab, imaging): Alert to potential delays
   - Operations: Prepare surge bed areas
2. **Increase triage vigilance:**
   - Confirm ESI placement for all patients
   - Identify potential ESI-4/5 candidates for diversion or fast track
3. **Begin contingency planning:**
   - Review mutual aid agreements
   - Identify available surge beds
   - Confirm staff call-in procedures

#### STEP 3: Phase 1 Surge Activation (Yellow threshold, 80–100%)
1. **ED director activates Phase 1 surge protocol:**
   - Communicate activation to all staff via overhead, secure messaging
   - Activate call-in of additional nursing staff (+20% baseline)
   - Open contingency bed areas (recovery room, observation unit, etc.)
2. **Implement flow management:**
   - Increase ESI triage frequency, confirm all patients appropriately triaged
   - Identify ESI-4/5 patients for potential discharge or referral to urgent care
   - Coordinate with admitting: Hold routine elective admissions if possible
3. **Optimize resource allocation:**
   - Fast-track ESI-4/5 patients to minimize bed occupancy time
   - Prioritize ED throughput: Expedite imaging, lab, disposition decisions
   - Coordinate with ICU, floor: Expedite bed availability for ED patients
4. **Monitor & report:**
   - Report to ED director every 30 minutes: Current census, acuity, arrival rate, resource utilization
   - Update forecast for next 12 hours

#### STEP 4: Phase 2 Surge Activation (Orange threshold, 100–120%)
1. **ED director escalates to Phase 2:**
   - Increase staffing by 40% of baseline (call-in additional RNs, technicians)
   - Open all surge bed areas (hallway beds, alternative care spaces)
   - Activate diversion for routine transfers to other hospitals
2. **Implement aggressive flow management:**
   - Confirm all ESI placements correct; consider ESI-3 for discharge if stable
   - Maximize fast-track throughput; consider off-site urgent care triage
   - Coordinate with floor: Expedite discharges to free beds
   - Partner with OR: Expedite operating room releases if available
3. **Supply chain management:**
   - Verify availability of critical supplies (IV fluids, medications, dressings)
   - Request additional supplies from central storage or mutual aid
4. **Communication & support:**
   - Brief all staff on surge status, expectations, resource constraints
   - Provide psychological support and breaks for staff as possible
   - Maintain communication with hospital leadership, referring physicians

#### STEP 5: Phase 3 Surge Activation (Red threshold, 120%+ capacity)
1. **ED director escalates to Phase 3:**
   - Increase staffing by 60%+ (activate all available staff, mutual aid, call medical staff)
   - Activate hospital disaster protocol if available
   - Request state/federal resources if projected to exceed local capacity
2. **Implement maximum surge measures:**
   - All diversion protocols active (no transfers accepted unless receiving-facility initiated)
   - Consider alternative care sites: Observation units, ambulatory surgery center, off-campus facilities
   - Coordinate with floor: Aggressive discharge, consider early discharge of stable patients
   - Partner with admissions: Restrict routine admissions; elective procedures on hold
3. **Resource reallocation:**
   - Redistribute physicians/advanced providers from other areas to ED as possible
   - Implement strict supply rationing protocols
   - Activate mutual aid and external resource requests
4. **Patient safety & documentation:**
   - Document surge status, resource constraints, and patient management decisions
   - Ensure all ESI placements are appropriate given constraints
   - Monitor for complications or adverse events; report immediately
5. **Command & control:**
   - Activate hospital incident command system (HICS) if not already active
   - Establish regular briefing cadence (15–30 min) with hospital leadership
   - Coordinate with public health, EMS, other hospitals on region-wide surge response

#### STEP 6: De-escalation (When census dropping below threshold, e.g., < 100%)
1. **ED director evaluates de-escalation trigger:**
   - Current census below threshold for 2+ hours?
   - Arrival rate stabilized or decreasing?
   - Forecast shows continued decline?
2. **Gradual de-escalation:**
   - Reduce call-in staffing (send non-essential staff home by seniority, self-volunteer first)
   - Release surge bed areas in reverse order of opening (last opened, first released)
   - Resume routine operations and elective admissions
   - Lift diversion protocols gradually (coordinate with other hospitals)
3. **Notify all staff:**
   - Communicate de-escalation status
   - Thank staff for surge participation
4. **Document surge event:**
   - Compile census log, resource utilization, staffing hours, incidents
   - Prepare for post-surge review

#### STEP 7: Post-Surge Review & Learning (Within 1 week)
1. **Compile surge documentation:**
   - Timeline of surge activation and de-escalation
   - Census log, acuity data, resource utilization
   - Staff feedback, incident reports, near-misses
2. **Conduct review with key stakeholders:**
   - ED leadership, nursing, operations, admitting, floor managers
   - Discuss: What went well? What needs improvement?
   - Identify protocol changes, training needs, process improvements
3. **Update baseline data:**
   - Adjust MTBF baselines for equipment used heavily during surge
   - Schedule preventive maintenance for high-utilization equipment
4. **Share findings:**
   - Communicate lessons learned to staff and hospital leadership
   - Update surge protocols as needed

## Workflow 3: Equipment Maintenance Planning

**Objective:** Establish baseline MTBF data, monitor equipment usage, predict failures, schedule preventive maintenance, and optimize equipment replacement cycles.

**Time Frame:** Ongoing, quarterly review cycles  
**Participants:** Biomedical/clinical engineering, ED director, operations, supply chain  
**Outcome:** Maintained equipment reliability, reduced downtime, optimized maintenance costs

### Detailed Steps

#### STEP 1: Equipment Inventory & Baseline Establishment (Initial setup, then annual refresh)
1. **Compile equipment inventory:**
   - List all critical ED equipment: Ventilators, cardiac monitors, infusion pumps, defibrillators, portable suction, patient beds, etc.
   - For each item: Model, serial number, purchase date, current operating hours (if available)
2. **Gather MTBF baseline data:**
   - Review manufacturer specifications for typical MTBF
   - Consult historical service records for facility equipment
   - Adjust baselines for facility-specific usage patterns (high-volume ED may have shorter MTBF)
3. **Document in maintenance schedule template:**
   - Create entry for each equipment item with: Equipment type, model, serial #, purchase date, MTBF baseline, last maintenance date, operating hours
   - Save in `planning/maintenance-schedule-template.md`
4. **Establish baseline metrics:**
   - Track: Total operating hours, number of failures, time between failures, maintenance costs

#### STEP 2: Usage Logging & Monitoring (Monthly)
1. **Log equipment operating hours:**
   - Ventilators: Record daily usage hours per patient, sum monthly
   - Cardiac monitors: Record bed hours (hour × number of units in operation)
   - Infusion pumps: Record daily operating hours across all units
   - Portable suction: Record number of uses, hours in operation
2. **Document service events:**
   - Any repairs, battery replacements, filter changes, recalibrations
   - Date, hours since last maintenance, work performed, technician
3. **Note failure events:**
   - Any unplanned downtime, failure mode, corrective action
4. **Update maintenance schedule** with current operating hours

#### STEP 3: Risk Calculation & Monitoring (Quarterly, or when Risk Score > 75%)
1. **Calculate risk score for each equipment:**
   - **Risk Score = (Operating Hours / MTBF) × 100**
   - Example: Ventilator with 3,500 operating hours and MTBF of 5,000 hrs = (3,500 / 5,000) × 100 = 70%
2. **Categorize risk:**
   - Green (0–50%): Normal operation, routine maintenance
   - Yellow (50–75%): Schedule maintenance within 2–4 weeks
   - Orange (75–90%): Prioritize maintenance within 1 week, consider backup unit
   - Red (90%+): Immediate maintenance or replacement within 24–48 hrs
3. **Flag high-risk equipment:**
   - Identify all equipment with Risk Score > 75%
   - Request biomedical engineering evaluation
   - Alert ED leadership to potential capacity constraints
4. **Monitor trending:**
   - Equipment steadily climbing toward MTBF threshold? → Plan replacement
   - Unexpected failure despite low risk score? → Investigate failure mode, adjust MTBF baseline

#### STEP 4: Preventive Maintenance Scheduling (When Risk Score 50–75%)
1. **Coordinate with biomedical engineering:**
   - Identify window for maintenance that minimizes ED disruption
   - Estimate maintenance duration and impact on equipment availability
   - Schedule maintenance 2–4 weeks in advance if possible
2. **Plan backup resources:**
   - Identify backup equipment or borrow from other departments
   - If no backup available, schedule maintenance during low-volume period
3. **Document pre-maintenance baseline:**
   - Current operating hours, date of maintenance, equipment condition notes
4. **Perform maintenance:**
   - Biomedical technician conducts preventive maintenance per manufacturer protocol
   - Replace wear parts (batteries, seals, filters) as indicated
   - Perform recalibration and testing
5. **Post-maintenance documentation:**
   - Document maintenance performed, parts replaced, any issues found
   - Reset equipment operating hours counter (or note maintenance completion)
   - Perform acceptance test; confirm equipment safe for clinical use
6. **Return to service:**
   - Update maintenance schedule with post-maintenance date and reset operating hours
   - Notify ED leadership equipment is back in service
   - Resume normal monitoring

#### STEP 5: High-Risk Equipment Management (When Risk Score 75–90%)
1. **Request urgent biomedical engineering evaluation:**
   - Schedule maintenance within 1 week
   - If maintenance cannot be scheduled, consider replacement or intensive monitoring
2. **Activate backup plan:**
   - Identify backup unit; bring into service immediately
   - If no backup available, reduce utilization or establish monitoring protocol
3. **Risk communication:**
   - Alert ED leadership to potential failure risk
   - Discuss contingency plans: Alternative care delivery, mutual aid, patient diversion
4. **Expedite replacement if needed:**
   - If failure likely before next maintenance window, procure replacement
   - Account for lead times: Ventilators (4–6 weeks), monitors (2–4 weeks), pumps (3–6 weeks)

#### STEP 6: Critical Risk Equipment Replacement (When Risk Score ≥ 90%)
1. **Immediate actions:**
   - Activate backup equipment immediately if available
   - If no backup available, restrict clinical use or prepare for patient transfer
   - Alert ED director and hospital leadership
2. **Expedite maintenance or replacement:**
   - Request emergency biomedical engineering response for maintenance
   - If maintenance not feasible, replace equipment immediately
   - Activate procurement fast-track if needed (expedited delivery)
3. **Supply chain coordination:**
   - Confirm replacement equipment availability and delivery timeline
   - Arrange emergency delivery if standard timeline inadequate
   - Brief staff on transition to new equipment
4. **Clinical notification:**
   - Inform all staff of equipment status, backup availability, usage restrictions

#### STEP 7: Replacement Planning (Long-term, annually)
1. **Analyze equipment lifespan data:**
   - Equipment approaching MTBF baseline consistently? → Plan replacement
   - Repeated failures despite maintenance? → Consider replacement
   - Technology outdated or parts unavailable? → Recommend replacement
2. **Develop replacement schedule:**
   - Identify equipment requiring replacement within next 12–24 months
   - Estimate costs and lead times
   - Prioritize based on criticality and failure risk
3. **Submit to capital planning:**
   - Request approval for replacement equipment budget
   - Include: Equipment type, business case (failure risk, performance improvement), cost, timeline
4. **Procurement & installation:**
   - Order replacement equipment
   - Coordinate with vendor for delivery and installation
   - Provide staff training on new equipment
   - Transfer data/settings from old equipment as applicable
5. **Decommissioning:**
   - Document final operating hours and condition of replaced equipment
   - Dispose properly (biomedical hazard, electrical recycling as applicable)
   - Close equipment record

#### STEP 8: Quarterly Review & Continuous Improvement
1. **Compile maintenance metrics:**
   - Total equipment operating hours (all equipment)
   - Preventive maintenance completion rate
   - Failure events and corrective actions
   - Maintenance costs
   - Equipment replacement forecast
2. **Analyze trends:**
   - Are equipment lifespans improving or declining?
   - Are maintenance schedules effective at preventing failures?
   - Are MTBF baselines accurate?
3. **Review with stakeholders:**
   - Biomedical engineering, ED leadership, operations, supply chain
   - Discuss: What's working? What needs adjustment?
   - Update MTBF baselines based on facility experience
4. **Adjust protocols:**
   - Update preventive maintenance intervals if needed
   - Revise risk scoring thresholds if trending data suggests changes
   - Plan replacements for next quarter

## Workflow 4: Training & Drill Execution

**Objective:** Design and conduct realistic triage training exercises, assess staff competency, provide performance feedback, and drive continuous improvement.

**Time Frame:** 2–4 hours per exercise (tabletop); 4–8 hours (full-scale)  
**Participants:** ED director, clinical staff, training coordinator, facilitators, observers  
**Outcome:** Trained staff, documented competency, identified improvement areas

### Detailed Steps

#### STEP 1: Drill Planning & Design (1–2 weeks prior)
1. **Define learning objectives:**
   - Protocol knowledge? (e.g., START algorithm mastery)
   - Operational flow? (e.g., triage area setup, patient routing)
   - Resource management? (e.g., surge escalation decisions)
   - Teamwork? (e.g., communication, coordination)
2. **Select exercise type:**
   - **Tabletop:** Seated discussion of scenario; 2–3 hours; low cost; good for decision-making
   - **Functional:** Simulation of specific functions (triage area setup, transport coordination); 3–4 hours
   - **Full-scale:** Complete MCI simulation with actors, equipment, transport; 6–8 hours; higher cost; most realistic
3. **Design scenario:**
   - Use `/prompts/mci-scenario-generator.md` to create realistic scenario
   - Specify: MCI type (vehicle crash, building collapse, weather event), location, estimated patient count, injury types
   - Define: Patient roster with demographics, vital signs, injuries, initial treatment needs
4. **Prepare materials:**
   - Print patient scenario cards or load into simulation software
   - Prepare observer forms with performance metrics
   - Develop facilitator guide with expected decisions and learning points
   - Organize equipment: Triage tags, bandages, assessment tools, patient mannequins (if full-scale)
5. **Recruit & brief participants:**
   - Identify participants (e.g., all ED nurses, triage team)
   - Brief on date, time, location, scenario overview
   - Clarify expectations: This is training, no performance pressure; debrief will be constructive
6. **Recruit facilitators & observers:**
   - Identify facilitators (experienced triage officers, educators)
   - Recruit observers (optional; useful for large exercises)
   - Brief on scoring criteria and observation methods

#### STEP 2: Pre-Exercise Briefing (30 minutes before start)
1. **Gather all participants:**
   - Confirm attendance and role assignments
   - Distribute role cards if applicable (e.g., if some participants playing patients)
2. **Present scenario:**
   - Incident type, time, location
   - Estimated number of patients, injury types
   - Available resources (equipment, staff, facility layout)
   - Expected protocol to use (START, SALT, ESI)
3. **Review learning objectives:**
   - What are we practicing today?
   - What success looks like
4. **Address questions:**
   - Clarify any scenario details
   - Confirm understanding of triage protocol
5. **Set expectations:**
   - Simulation will be realistic but forgiving
   - Facilitators will pause if safety concerns arise
   - Debrief will focus on learning, not criticism
   - All decisions will be reviewed constructively

#### STEP 3: Exercise Execution

**For Tabletop Exercise:**
1. Facilitator presents scenario: "You're the ED director. You receive a call at 2 PM: 'Multi-vehicle crash on Highway 101. Fire department reports ~15–20 patients, multiple injuries. ETA to hospital: 20 minutes.'"
2. Participants discuss and role-play decisions:
   - Should we activate surge protocol? Which phase?
   - What triage location will we use?
   - Who do we call for mutual aid?
   - How do we staff the triage area?
3. Facilitator guides discussion, injects additional information:
   - "First ambulance arriving now, 3 patients: one unresponsive, one with severe leg fracture, one walking with minor injuries. Triage decision?"
   - Participants apply START/SALT protocol, categorize patients
   - Facilitator provides feedback: "That's correct per START algorithm; patient had respirations of 8, so Immediate category. Good assessment."
4. Exercise progresses through key decision points (escalation, resource allocation, communication)
5. Continue until scenario resolution or time limit reached

**For Full-Scale Exercise:**
1. Simulate incident scene:
   - Set up triage area with supplies
   - Stationed "patients" (trained actors or staff volunteers with injury makeup)
   - Realistic transport, staging areas
2. Participants role-play actual response:
   - Incident commander establishes scene command
   - Triage team sets up triage area, applies protocol to arriving patients
   - Treatment areas receive categorized patients, deliver care
   - Transport coordinates patient movement to hospital
3. Observers document performance:
   - Triage accuracy: Did staff apply protocol correctly?
   - Timing: How long per patient? Were thresholds met?
   - Communication: Was information clearly communicated?
   - Resource utilization: Was allocation appropriate?
4. Facilitators monitor for safety, step in if actual harm could occur
5. Continue until all patients processed or time limit reached

#### STEP 4: Real-Time Feedback & Coaching
1. **As exercise progresses:**
   - Facilitators observe key decisions
   - Note errors: Protocol misapplication, communication gaps, timing issues
   - Provide coaching questions: "What vital sign did START say to assess next?"
   - Encourage self-correction where appropriate
2. **After key decision points:**
   - Pause and debrief: "That patient presented with RR of 35, BP 85, and altered mental status. What category per START?"
   - Correct misapplication: "Actually, per START Step 3 (mental status assessment), altered mental status = Immediate (Red). Let's continue."

#### STEP 5: Exercise Closure & Transition to Debrief
1. **Call end of exercise:**
   - Facilitator: "Exercise end state reached. Excellent work, everyone. We'll reconvene for debrief in 10 minutes."
2. **Gather materials:**
   - Collect observer forms, patient scenario cards, any other documentation
   - Note timing, patient counts, triage distribution

#### STEP 6: After-Action Debrief (1–1.5 hours)
1. **Gather participants & observers:**
   - Seated discussion format, informal atmosphere
   - Make clear: This is a learning conversation, not a performance evaluation
2. **Facilitator guide discussion using "What / So What / Now What" framework:**

   **WHAT HAPPENED?**
   - Facilitator: "Let's talk about the exercise. What did you observe happening?"
   - Participants share objective observations: "We triaged 18 patients in about 35 minutes."
   - Facilitate discussion of key events and decisions

   **SO WHAT?**
   - Facilitator: "Why does that matter? What were we trying to accomplish?"
   - Link observations to learning objectives: "We wanted to practice START protocol. Did we apply it correctly?"
   - Review specific decisions: "When we assessed that patient with RR of 25, we categorized as Delayed. Per START, what should we have assessed next?"
   - Celebrate successes: "Excellent communication between triage and treatment area. That coordination kept the flow moving."
   - Address gaps: "A few patients were misclassified initially. Let's look at what happened in those cases."

   **NOW WHAT?**
   - Facilitator: "What will we do differently based on what we learned?"
   - Participants suggest improvements:
     - "I'll review the mental status assessment step before next exercise."
     - "We should designate a clear communication point between triage and transport."
     - "We need more training on pediatric vital signs (for JumpSTART)."
   - Facilitator helps prioritize and assign ownership for improvements

3. **Structured feedback (if using observer forms):**
   - Review key metrics:
     - Triage accuracy: % correct classifications
     - Timing: Average seconds per patient, total triage time
     - Communication: Clear exchange of information? Any confusion?
     - Resource utilization: Appropriate staffing and equipment?
   - Discuss trends: "As patient volume increased, triage time decreased. That suggests good efficiency but let's ensure accuracy wasn't compromised."

4. **Protocol review (if errors identified):**
   - Revisit START/JumpSTART/SALT decision tree for protocol misapplication
   - Facilitate group discussion: "Why is this step important? What would happen if we skipped it?"
   - Provide correct decision-making: "When respiratory rate is normal but mental status altered, START says the patient is Immediate (Red). Remember: any abnormal finding = Immediate."

5. **Competency assessment:**
   - If exercise was designed to assess competency:
     - Review individual performance against facility standards
     - Identify staff requiring remedial training: Scoring < 80% on key decisions
     - Schedule follow-up training within 30 days
   - Communicate results to participants and ED director

#### STEP 7: Documentation & Learning Logging
1. **Compile exercise documentation:**
   - Observer forms, participant feedback, triage decisions made
   - Photographs (if allowed) or video (if recorded)
   - Timing data, patient volume, resource utilization
2. **Complete session log:**
   - Date, exercise type (tabletop / functional / full-scale)
   - Objectives, scenario description
   - Participants and attendance
   - Key observations, lessons learned
   - Recommended follow-up actions
   - Save in `work-log/session-log.md`
3. **Competency records:**
   - If competency assessment: Document results for each participant
   - Send individual feedback to participants
   - Alert ED director to any staff requiring remedial training
4. **Share findings:**
   - Communicate lessons learned to all ED staff (whether they participated or not)
   - Highlight improvement areas and planned actions
   - Celebrate successes

#### STEP 8: Follow-Up & Continuous Improvement (Within 2 weeks)
1. **Implement improvement actions:**
   - Prioritized improvements identified in debrief
   - Assign owners and target dates
   - Examples: Protocol refresher training, process changes, equipment procurement
2. **Remedial training (if competency gaps identified):**
   - Identify staff scoring < 80%
   - Schedule one-on-one or small group coaching
   - Re-assess competency after training
3. **Plan next exercise:**
   - Schedule follow-up exercise (recommend quarterly)
   - Adjust scenario or focus based on current learning needs
   - Involve different staff or departments as needed
