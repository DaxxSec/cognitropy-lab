# MCI Scale Definitions & Incident Classification

## Mass Casualty Incident Definitions

### NFPA 901 (National Fire Protection Association) MCI Classification

**MASS CASUALTY INCIDENT (MCI):**
An incident involving three or more patients, which by the factors of numbers, types, or severity of illness or injury, requires extraordinary resources to manage.

### National Disaster Life Support (NDLS) Casualty Scale

| Scale | Total Patients | Immediate | Delayed | Minor |
|-------|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 1** | 10–50 | 1–5 | 3–20 | 5–30 |
| **Level 2** | 50–100 | 5–15 | 20–50 | 30–50 |
| **Level 3** | 100–300 | 15–50 | 50–150 | 50–150 |
| **Level 4** | 300–1,000 | 50–200 | 150–500 | 150–400 |
| **Level 5** | 1,000+ | 200+ | 500+ | 400+ |

### Casualty Triage Categories

| Category | Color | Priority | Definition |
|----------|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Immediate** | Red | 1 (Urgent) | Life-threatening injuries; salvageable with immediate intervention |
| **Delayed** | Yellow | 2 (Urgent) | Serious injuries; can wait for treatment; stable for now |
| **Minor** | Green | 3 (Walking Wounded) | Minor injuries; self-care or minimal intervention |
| **Expectant** | Black | 4 (Comfort Care) | Non-survivable injuries in resource-constrained setting |
| **Deceased** | Black (with death sticker) | — | Already dead at scene or before arrival |

## MCI Activation Criteria

### Hospital Declares MCI When:

1. **Patient Volume Exceeds Normal Capacity**
   - Routine ED capacity: 80 patients/day = ~3.3/hour
   - MCI trigger: Sudden influx >10 patients within 1–2 hours
   - Example: 15 patients arriving within 30 minutes = MCI activation

2. **Acuity Mix Overwhelming**
   - Normal: ~15% ESI-1, 35% ESI-2, 50% other
   - MCI trigger: >50% critically ill/injured (ESI-1 & 2 combined)
   - Example: 12 of 18 patients need ICU-level care = MCI

3. **External Incident Report**
   - EMS/Fire dispatch reports "mass casualty incident"
   - Police reports "multiple victims" or "major accident"
   - Public health alerts (disease outbreak, chemical exposure)

4. **Multiple Critical Injuries from Single Incident**
   - Vehicle collision with 4+ severe injuries
   - Building collapse with entrapment
   - Explosion or fire with multiple burns
   - Workplace or mass gathering incident with traumatic injuries

5. **Resource Depletion Risk**
   - ICU beds would be filled by one incident's patients
   - OR would be unavailable for hours due to volume
   - Blood bank or specific medication supply critical shortage
   - Staff unavailable due to off-duty or call-in delays

## Incident Type Classifications

### Vehicle-Related MCIs

**Characteristics:**
- Multi-vehicle collisions (highway vs. urban)
- High-speed impacts; multiple mechanisms
- Crush injuries, traumatic brain injuries, internal bleeding
- Predictable patient mix: ~20–30% Immediate, 40–50% Delayed, 20–30% Minor

**Examples:**
- "4-vehicle pile-up on I-70, 15 patients"
- "School bus collision, 25 children, mixed injuries"
- "Drunk driver hit pedestrians at shopping center, 8 patients"

---

### Structural/Building MCIs

**Characteristics:**
- Building collapse, partial collapse, structural failure
- Entrapment (requires extrication time = delay before triage)
- Crush injuries, traumatic amputations, severe multi-trauma
- Prolonged scene time (hours for extrication)
- Higher proportion of Immediate/Expectant patients

**Examples:**
- "Building collapse during construction, estimated 20–30 trapped"
- "Earthquake, partial collapse of shopping mall"
- "Roof collapse during event, 50+ patients"

**Special Considerations:**
- Delayed arrival as extrication progresses
- Prolonged entrapment may worsen outcomes (crush syndrome)
- Expectant category may increase as time passes
- Scene coordination critical (EMS, fire, rescue, police)

---

### Mass Gathering & Crowd-Related MCIs

**Characteristics:**
- Events (concerts, sports, festivals, gatherings)
- Crowd crush, trampling injuries, falls from height
- Panic-related injuries, psychological distress
- Predictable location (venue)
- Rapid patient influx to nearest hospital(s)

**Examples:**
- "Concert venue crowd crush, 100+ injuries"
- "Stadium evacuation, evacuation injuries"
- "Stampede at event, 50+ patients"

**Special Considerations:**
- Significant volume of Minor injuries (crowd panic, falls, psychological distress)
- Wide geographic dispersal to multiple hospitals
- Coordination with mutual aid essential
- Follow-up psychiatric support needed

---

### Natural Disaster MCIs

**Characteristics:**
- Earthquake, tornado, hurricane, flood, wildfire
- Widespread damage; prolonged scene presence (hours to days)
- Variable injury types (trauma, environmental exposure, secondary injuries)
- Potential for overwhelming EMS and hospital systems regionally
- Coordination across multiple hospitals and jurisdictions

**Examples:**
- "Earthquake, 6.5 magnitude, estimated 500+ injuries across county"
- "Tornado strike, 100+ homes destroyed, 200+ transported to area hospitals"
- "Wildfire evacuation, 50+ patients with burns and inhalation injuries"

**Special Considerations:**
- Regional impact; coordination with state/federal resources
- Mutual aid agreements critical
- Prolonged sustainability needed (not single-event spike)
- Environmental factors (heat, cold, contamination)

---

### Biological/Chemical/Radiological MCIs

**Characteristics:**
- Disease outbreak, toxic exposure, chemical spill, radiation event
- Ongoing risk (contamination, exposure risk continues)
- Decontamination required before hospital arrival
- Specialized treatment protocols
- Psychological distress (fear of exposure, delayed symptoms)

**Examples:**
- "Chemical plant explosion, unknown substance released, 30+ exposed"
- "Disease outbreak alert: 50+ suspected cases transported"
- "Train derailment with hazmat spill, evacuation zone established"

**Special Considerations:**
- Scene decontamination may delay patient arrival
- Isolation/infection control protocols activated
- Specialized treatment protocols (antidotes, chelation, etc.)
- Public health authority involvement
- Media/public communication critical

---

### Workplace MCIs

**Characteristics:**
- Industrial accident, construction site collapse, machinery injury
- Occupational exposures (toxic, electrical, heat-related)
- Known hazards and potential contamination
- Worker's compensation implications

**Examples:**
- "Construction site collapse, 8 workers trapped, 3 transported"
- "Chemical facility explosion, 15+ workers injured or exposed"
- "Electrical hazard incident, multiple burn injuries"

**Special Considerations:**
- Occupational Safety & Health Administration (OSHA) involvement
- Workers' compensation reporting required
- Workplace safety investigation needed
- Coordination with employer

## Incident Severity Indicators

### Low Severity (Routine Response)
- 3–5 patients total
- Mostly Minor and Delayed injuries
- Scene stable, no ongoing hazard
- Single hospital response adequate
- **Hospital Action:** Notify ED, monitor capacity; standard protocol may be sufficient

### Moderate Severity (Activation of Surge Protocol Phase 1)
- 6–20 patients
- Mix of Immediate, Delayed, Minor (no Expectant)
- Scene secured, no ongoing hazard
- Single hospital response adequate
- **Hospital Action:** Activate Phase 1 surge, call-in staff (+20%), prepare surge beds

### High Severity (Phase 2 Surge Activation)
- 20–50 patients
- Significant number of Immediate (>20%)
- Some Expectant patients likely
- May exceed single hospital capacity; mutual aid needed
- **Hospital Action:** Activate Phase 2 surge (+40% staff), request mutual aid, possible patient diversion

### Critical/Major Disaster (Phase 3 Surge + State/Federal Assistance)
- 50–100+ patients
- High percentage Immediate and Expectant
- Regional impact; multiple hospital coordination required
- State/federal emergency management activated
- **Hospital Action:** Phase 3 surge (maximum activation), mutual aid, state resources, possible evacuation

### Catastrophic (Mass Casualty Disaster Response)
- 100+ patients
- Widespread region affected
- Significant Expectant category
- Hospital may be damaged; evacuation possible
- **Hospital Action:** Full disaster response, state/federal coordination, mutual aid, possible evacuation/relocation

## Resource Requirements by MCI Scale

### Level 1 (10–50 Patients) — Single Hospital Response

| Resource | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **Hospital Surge Phase** | Phase 1–2 |
| **Additional RN Staff** | +20–40% |
| **Physicians** | +1–2 additional |
| **ICU Beds** | 2–5 available |
| **OR Availability** | 1–2 ORs for critical cases |
| **ED Capacity Used** | 50–80% |
| **Mutual Aid** | Prepare if exceeding capacity |
| **Activation Level** | Hospital-level MCI response |

### Level 2 (50–100 Patients) — Regional Mutual Aid

| Resource | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **Hospital Surge Phase** | Phase 2–3 |
| **Additional Staff** | +40–60% across all roles |
| **ICU Beds** | 5–15 available (multiple hospitals) |
| **OR Availability** | 2–3 ORs |
| **ED Capacity** | 80%+ of regional capacity |
| **Mutual Aid** | Multiple hospitals activated |
| **Coordination** | Hospital incident command + Regional coordination |

### Level 3–5 (100+ Patients) — Regional/State/Federal Disaster Response

| Resource | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **Surge Phase** | Phase 3 maximum + Alternate care sites |
| **Staff** | +60%+ plus mutual aid from other hospitals/regions |
| **Specialized Resources** | NDMS teams, military medical, federal assistance |
| **Coordination** | State emergency management, federal (FEMA) if needed |
| **Activation** | Public Health Emergency declaration |

## Incident Management Decision Points

### Decision 1: Activate MCI Protocol?
**Trigger:** 3+ patients from single incident OR abnormal acuity mix
**Action:** Notify incident commander, activate hospital emergency operations center (EOC)

### Decision 2: Single vs. Multi-Hospital Response?
**Trigger:** Patient volume + acuity assessment
**Action:** If exceeding capacity, request mutual aid to distribute patients

### Decision 3: External Mutual Aid Needed?
**Trigger:** Exceeding surge Phase 2 capacity OR sustained demand
**Action:** Activate mutual aid agreements, request resources from neighboring hospitals/EMS

### Decision 4: State/Federal Assistance?
**Trigger:** Exceeding regional capacity (100+ patients likely) OR special hazards (CBRN, biological)
**Action:** Contact state health department, activate emergency management, request NDMS teams

### Decision 5: Evacuation/Relocation?
**Trigger:** Facility damage, hazardous environment, overwhelmed capacity
**Action:** Determine safe receiving facilities, coordinate evacuation, maintain continuity of care

## Documentation & Reporting

**Incident Report Must Include:**
- Date, time, location, incident type
- Total patients (by category: Immediate, Delayed, Minor, Expectant, Deceased)
- ETA of first patient, last patient
- Receiving hospital(s)
- Resources activated (surge phase, mutual aid, state/federal)
- Outcomes (admitted, discharged, transferred, expired)
- Lessons learned, improvement actions

**Regulatory Reporting:**
- Hospital incident command system (HICS) documentation
- State health department notification (if required)
- OSHA reporting (if workplace incident)
- Public health reporting (if disease-related)
