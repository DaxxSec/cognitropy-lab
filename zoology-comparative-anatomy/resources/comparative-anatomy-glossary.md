# Comparative Anatomy — Working Glossary

Authoritative quick-reference for terms the agent must use precisely. Citations to canonical sources are given where definitions originate or have been refined.

## Homology, Analogy, Homoplasy

- **Homology** — Similarity of structure due to **shared ancestry**. Operationally diagnosed by topology (same position relative to invariant landmarks), ontogeny (same developmental origin), and phylogenetic congruence. (Patterson 1982; Hall 1994; Wagner 2014.)
- **Analogy** — Similarity of function without shared ancestry of the structure itself.
- **Homoplasy** — Independent acquisition of similar character state in unrelated lineages.
  - **Convergence** — Independent origin under similar selective pressure (camera-eye in cephalopods + vertebrates).
  - **Parallelism** — Independent origin from a similar precursor (multiple origins of the mammalian patella from common tendon-ossification potential).
  - **Reversal** — Loss-then-regain of an ancestral state (true loss of teeth followed by re-acquisition is rare but documented in some frogs).
- **Serial homology** — Correspondence between repeated structures within an individual organism (e.g. between cervical and thoracic vertebrae). Same individual ≠ same lineage history; serial-homologous structures across taxa carry both serial and historical homology relations.
- **Special homology** vs. **General homology** — Special: the specific structure (e.g. the human humerus is specially homologous with the bat humerus). General: a feature is general homology when present in many descendants of a common ancestor (e.g. notochord is a general homology of chordates).

## Phylogenetic Character Categories

- **Synapomorphy** — Shared **derived** character state. Defines a clade.
- **Plesiomorphy** — Ancestral character state.
- **Symplesiomorphy** — Shared ancestral state. **Useless** for resolving clade relationships; do not confuse with synapomorphy.
- **Autapomorphy** — Unique derived state of one taxon. Identifies the taxon but doesn't group it with others.
- **Polarity** — Directionality of state change (which is ancestral vs. derived). Determined by **outgroup comparison**.

## Anatomical Position & Direction

- **Anterior / Posterior** = toward head / tail (some texts use rostral/caudal).
- **Dorsal / Ventral** = toward back / belly. Invariant.
- **Medial / Lateral** = toward midline / away from midline. Midline = midsagittal plane.
- **Proximal / Distal** = toward attachment / away (used for limbs, projecting structures).
- **Cranial / Caudal** = toward head / tail. Preferred over anterior/posterior for quadrupeds.
- **Superior / Inferior** = upward / downward — only meaningful for upright humans; avoid in non-human anatomy.
- **Superficial / Deep** = toward skin / interior. Self-explanatory.
- **Reference planes** — sagittal (left/right), coronal (= frontal; dorsal/ventral), transverse (= horizontal; cranial/caudal).

## Skeletal Terminology

- **Foramen** — natural opening in bone transmitting nerve/vessel; identify what passes through.
- **Process** — projection from bone; specify base location.
- **Tubercle** — small bony prominence; **tuberosity** — large bony prominence.
- **Fossa** — depression.
- **Sulcus** — groove; identify what occupies/produces it.
- **Crest / Ridge** — elongate prominence.
- **Suture** — fibrous joint between bones (cranial); distinct from synchondrosis (cartilaginous), synostosis (fused).
- **Apophysis** — projection developed from a separate ossification center.
- **Diaphysis / Epiphysis** — long bone shaft / end with separate ossification.

## Ontogenetic Vocabulary

- **Cephalization** — concentration of sensory/CNS toward the anterior end during evolution.
- **Heterochrony** — change in developmental timing; subtypes:
  - **Pedomorphosis** — retention of juvenile features in adult (paedomorphic taxa).
    - **Neoteny** — retarded somatic development relative to reproductive (axolotl).
    - **Progenesis** — accelerated reproductive maturation relative to somatic.
    - **Postdisplacement** — delayed onset of development of a feature.
  - **Peramorphosis** — exaggerated development beyond ancestral adult condition.
    - **Hypermorphosis** — extended developmental period.
    - **Acceleration** — increased rate of development.
    - **Predisplacement** — earlier onset of development of a feature.

## Tooth Vocabulary (Mammals)

- **Tribosphenic molar pattern** — three principal cusps on each upper and lower molar:
  - Upper: paracone, protocone, metacone (+ hypocone in primates and others).
  - Lower: paraconid, protoconid, metaconid (+ entoconid, hypoconid).
- **Heterodonty** — differentiated tooth classes (incisors, canines, premolars, molars).
- **Diphyodont** — two tooth generations (deciduous + permanent).
- **Polyphyodont** — continuous tooth replacement (most non-mammalian vertebrates).
- **Acrodont / pleurodont / thecodont** — attachment styles.
- **Bunodont / lophodont / selenodont** — cusp form patterns.

## Phylogenetic / Cladistic Vocabulary

- **Monophyletic** (clade) — group containing common ancestor + all descendants. The only "natural" group in cladistics.
- **Paraphyletic** — group containing common ancestor but missing some descendants ("Reptilia" excluding Aves is paraphyletic).
- **Polyphyletic** — group not including the most recent common ancestor of its members. Avoid.
- **Sister group** — closest related clade.
- **Crown group** — clade of all extant members of a group + their most recent common ancestor + all its descendants.
- **Stem group** — extinct lineages outside the crown but more closely related to the crown than to the next-closest extant group.

## Morphometric Vocabulary

- **Type I landmark** — discrete juxtaposition of tissues (suture intersection, foramen center). Highest replicability.
- **Type II landmark** — local geometric property (point of maximum curvature). Medium replicability.
- **Type III landmark** — extremal point relative to another landmark. Lowest replicability.
- **Semilandmark** — sliding landmark along a curve, typically used in 3D Procrustes-based analysis to capture curve shape.
- **Procrustes superimposition** — translation, rotation, and scaling to standardize coordinate space across specimens. Foundation of geometric morphometrics.

## Common Misuses To Avoid

- "Camera-eye is homologous between cephalopods and vertebrates" — **homoplasy**, not homology.
- "Tail vertebrae are homologous to neck vertebrae" — they are **serial homologues** within an individual, but as taxonomic characters they are different positional categories.
- "All wings are homologous" — bat / bird / pterosaur wings are **homologous** as tetrapod forelimbs but **homoplastic** as wings (each independent flight evolution).
- "The ratite condition is plesiomorphic" — flight loss in ratites is **secondarily lost** (apomorphic loss); the absence of flight in ratites is not the ancestral condition of birds.
- "Whales are fish" — whales are **mammals**; their fish-like body shape is a convergent (homoplastic) consequence of marine secondary aquatic life.

## Pointers to Invertebrate-Phylum References

For non-vertebrate work, this glossary is insufficient. Pull from:

- **Brusca & Brusca** *Invertebrates* (3rd ed., 2003) — comprehensive across phyla.
- **Ruppert, Fox, Barnes** *Invertebrate Zoology* (7th ed., 2004).
