# Wildfire Management & Containment — Core Concepts

Background the agent reads before acting. Optimised for fast recall under time pressure, not exhaustive fire science. Where a number drives a decision tree, it lives in `references.md`.

## The Fire Behavior Triangle

Three factors govern how a wildfire behaves. Every size-up, projection, and triage call traces back to them:

- **Fuel** — the only leg humans can directly manipulate (line construction, removal, burnout). Characterized by *loading* (tons/acre), *arrangement* (ground/surface/aerial; continuous vs. patchy), *size class* (1-hr/10-hr/100-hr/1000-hr time-lag fuels), and *moisture content*. Fine dead fuels (1-hr, <¼") drive ignition and rate of spread; they respond to humidity within an hour.
- **Weather** — the most dynamic and dangerous leg. Wind (speed + direction + gustiness) is the dominant driver of spread and the cause of most blow-ups; relative humidity and temperature set fuel moisture; instability (see Haines) governs plume/column development.
- **Topography** — the constant. **Slope** accelerates upslope spread (fire preheats fuel above it); **aspect** sets fuel moisture and cure state (south/southwest aspects in the N. hemisphere are hotter, drier, more receptive); **terrain features** (chimneys, saddles, box canyons, narrow drainages) concentrate and align fire and are classic entrapment geography.

Rule of thumb: fire spreads fastest **up-slope and down-wind**; alignment of slope, wind, and a drainage is the setup for a wind-driven run.

## Anatomy of a Fire

Standard vocabulary for describing a fire's perimeter — triage and line assignments depend on naming parts unambiguously:

- **Origin** — point of ignition. **Heel (rear)** — the slow-spreading end, usually back toward the origin/into the wind; the normal **anchor point** for safe line construction.
- **Head** — the fastest-moving, most intense end; spreads with wind/slope. Most growth and danger.
- **Flanks (left/right)** — sides connecting heel to head, named as if standing at the heel looking toward the head.
- **Fingers** — long narrow projections off the main fire; the unburned pockets between them are dangerous (can be overrun).
- **Spot fires** — new ignitions ahead of the main fire from windborne embers (firebrands); **spotting** can leap control lines and is a primary reason containment fails.
- **Slop-over (breakover)** — fire crossing a control line at a point. **Green** = unburned fuel; **black** = burned area ("one foot in the black" / anchoring in the black is a core safety habit).

## Fire Type by Strata

- **Ground fire** — burns in subsurface organic material (duff, peat, roots); smoldering, hard to fully extinguish — the reason mop-up exists.
- **Surface fire** — burns surface litter, grass, shrubs; most fires, most line-building decisions.
- **Crown fire** — burns the forest canopy. **Passive** (torching, single trees), **active** (canopy + surface carry together — a wall of fire), **independent** (crown alone, ahead of surface). Crown fire is generally beyond direct suppression — the correct decision is almost always indirect line + structure triage + disengagement, not a frontal fight.

## Suppression Terminology

- **Fireline / control line / containment line** — a barrier (constructed, natural, or burned) intended to stop spread. **Handline** (hand tools to mineral soil), **dozer line**, **wet line**, **retardant/foam line**, **road or natural barrier**.
- **Anchor point** — a barrier (road, rockslide, the black) from which line construction begins so fire can't flank crews. *Always start from an anchor* — building line without one is a Watch Out.
- **Direct attack** — work the fire's edge directly (knock down, line at the heat). Viable only when flame length and intensity allow people/equipment at the edge.
- **Indirect attack** — build line at a distance (a defensible feature) and often **burn out** the fuel between line and fire. Used when the edge is too hot, terrain too steep, or to take advantage of a road/ridge.
- **Parallel attack** — line a short distance from the edge, splitting the difference; burn out the slop-ins.
- **Burnout / backfire** — intentionally removing fuel between the line and the fire. Burnout (tactical, near the line) vs. backfire (larger, to rob the main fire of energy — high-risk, IC-authorized).

## Containment Lifecycle

A fire moves through reportable states — these are the milestones a containment plan drives toward:

1. **Contained (% containment)** — a control line is *completed* around some fraction of the perimeter and is expected to hold under prevailing conditions. "40% contained" = 40% of the perimeter has finished, holding line.
2. **Controlled** — the entire perimeter has secured line, all spots are dealt with, and no further spread is expected.
3. **Mop-up** — extinguishing/cooling everything within a gridded distance of the line (cold-trailing by bare hand for heat); the slow, unglamorous work that prevents rekindle.
4. **Out / declared out** — no heat detected; the incident is closed.

## Structure Triage (WUI)

When fire threatens the wildland-urban interface, structures are triaged into four categories (NWCG / IRPG). The category drives whether and how resources engage:

- **Defensible — Stand Alone (Prep & Leave for action):** survivable space and structure hardening sufficient that the home will likely survive without on-site forces. Patrol, don't commit.
- **Defensible — Prep and Hold:** firefighters can safely improve and defend it during fire front passage *and have a safety zone*. The primary "engage" category.
- **Non-Defensible — Prep and Leave:** little can be done safely before the front; do quick prep (move fuels, close windows) and leave before the fire arrives. Do **not** stay through the front.
- **Non-Defensible — Rescue Drive-By:** unsafe to commit any resources; structure left to its fate. Resources used only for life rescue, not property.

The triage decision hinges on **safety zone availability, escape routes, time before the front, survivable space, construction/roofing, and access** — never on the value of the property.

## Fire Weather Metrics That Drive Decisions

- **Relative humidity (RH)** & **temperature** — low RH (esp. <15-20%) + high temp = receptive fine fuels. Watch the **crossover** (RH% ≤ temperature in °C/°F threshold per local rule) as a red flag.
- **Wind** — speed, sustained vs. gust, and *direction shifts* (frontal passages, foehn/Santa Ana/Diablo/Chinook winds). A forecast wind shift is the single most important thing to brief.
- **Fuel moisture** — dead (1-hr/10-hr) responds to weather fast; live fuel moisture (LFM) seasonal. Below critical thresholds, expect active fire behavior (see `references.md`).
- **Haines Index (1-6)** — atmospheric stability + moisture; 5-6 = dry/unstable = large plume-dominated fire potential.
- **Energy Release Component (ERC)** — NFDRS index of available energy / drought; high ERC = resistant-to-control fire.
- **Red Flag Warning** — NWS product: critical fire-weather (some mix of low RH, high wind, dry fuels, or dry lightning) expected. Triggers heightened posture.

## Common Failure Modes

These are the conditions that turn routine fires into fatality fires — the "Common Denominators of Fire Behavior on Tragedy Fires" and the Watch Outs:

- **Building line without an anchor point** — fire flanks the crew. Always anchor.
- **No LCES** — committing without lookouts, working comms, identified escape routes, and a safety zone of adequate size. The most common entrapment precursor.
- **Defending non-defensible structures** — staying through front passage at a home with no safety zone; the structure becomes the trap.
- **Underestimating spotting** — short-range spotting builds new heads behind the crew; the fire is suddenly all around you. Relatively small fires, in light fuels (grass), on slopes <30% — the deceptive "tragedy fire" profile.
- **Wind shift not briefed** — a flank becomes a head when the wind turns; crews on the old flank are now in front of the fire.
- **Downhill/indirect line construction toward the fire** — the high-risk Downhill Line Construction Checklist exists for a reason.
- **Late evacuation** — triggering Ready/Set/Go after the road is already threatened; evacuees and responders trapped together on the only egress.

## Operating Constraints

- Outputs are **advisory** to a qualified IC under ICS/NIMS and the AHJ — never authorization.
- The **10 Standard Firefighting Orders** and **18 Watch Out Situations** are hard constraints, not guidelines; no tactic that violates them is valid.
- Resource recommendations must respect **red-card qualifications** and jurisdictional/mutual-aid authority.
- Evacuation **orders** are a law-enforcement/AHJ legal function; this workspace recommends trigger points only.
- All fire-behavior projections are best-effort under uncertainty — state the fuel model, weather source/time, slope, and aspect, and treat BehavePlus/FSPro-class tools as the authoritative modeling check.
