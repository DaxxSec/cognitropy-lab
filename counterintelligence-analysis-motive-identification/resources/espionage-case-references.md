# Espionage Case Indicator-Pattern References

> Sanitized indicator-pattern observations from public sources (court records, declassified DOD/IC histories, the unclassified PERSEREC archive, and academic literature). Use as a reference for pattern recognition during indicator-checklist runs and motive profiling. **None of this material is operational; it is for analytic education.**

## How to Read This Reference

For each case, the entry summarizes:
- **Predominant motive** as determined by the public record (one or more MICE-RC categories)
- **Key indicator domains** that, with hindsight, were most diagnostic
- **Pattern lessons** — what the case teaches about indicator-pattern recognition

The reference is for *pattern* learning, not narrative re-litigation. Do not import these patterns as templates onto current cases; every case is its own indicator base. Use these as a calibration set for what the 5-domain checklist looks like in cases that turned out to be true positives.

## Cases

### Aldrich H. Ames (CIA, compromise period 1985–1994)
- **Predominant motive:** Money (acute), with ego/recognition secondary
- **Key indicator domains:** Financial (dominant), Foreign Contacts (later), Lifestyle (acute pre-compromise)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Financial-domain indicators were available years before detection (sudden large purchases inconsistent with salary, repayment of long-standing debts)
  - The financial indicator was missed in part because no one was running the standardized inspection across the cleared population
  - The lesson is *systematic* application of the financial domain across the cleared population, not just per-case investigation

### Robert P. Hanssen (FBI, compromise period 1979–2001)
- **Predominant motive:** Money + Ego mix
- **Key indicator domains:** Technical/Access (out-of-channel database queries on himself and his case), Financial (less acute than Ames but present)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - The technical/access indicator pattern (querying compromised cases, querying his own name in CI databases) was the key diagnostic
  - Indicator existed in audit logs for years; aggregation across domains and over time would have caught it
  - Self-initiated volunteer pattern (no recruiter; subject approached the foreign service) means the recruit-vector frame would have shown no RASCLS pattern — illustrating that motive analysis still applies in the absence of an external recruiter

### John Walker / Walker Spy Ring (USN, compromise period 1968–1985)
- **Predominant motive:** Money (primary), with family-network expansion (Liking + Money for recruited family members)
- **Key indicator domains:** Financial (sustained), Foreign Contacts (operational tradecraft)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Motive can extend through a network — a primary subject can recruit secondary subjects on a Money + Liking basis
  - Long-running compromises produce sustained financial indicators, not single events
  - The case prompted CI doctrine to extend motive analysis from the subject to the subject's close associates

### Jonathan J. Pollard (US Naval Intelligence, compromise period 1984–1985)
- **Predominant motive:** Ideology (Israel as third-party recipient), Money (secondary)
- **Key indicator domains:** Ideological, Foreign Contacts, Technical/Access (anomalous compartment requests)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Ideology toward an ally still produces unauthorized disclosure
  - Ideological indicators in this case were observable (statements, advocacy) but were not weighted because the recipient state was not perceived as "hostile"
  - The framework correction: ideology applies to *unauthorized* disclosure regardless of recipient state's relationship to the employer state

### Ana Belén Montes (DIA, compromise period 1985–2001)
- **Predominant motive:** Ideology (Cuba)
- **Key indicator domains:** Ideological, Foreign Contacts, Lifestyle (secrecy patterns)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Ideological commitment can be sustained across decades with low operational tradecraft
  - Periodic re-examination of long-tenured cleared personnel against the full checklist is the doctrinal lesson
  - Indicator pattern was recognizable in retrospect but was not flagged because the subject had cultivated reputation as a model employee — illustrating the halo-effect failure mode the peer-review checklist guards against

### Kendall Myers / Gwendolyn Myers (State Department, compromise period 1979–2007)
- **Predominant motive:** Ideology (Cuba)
- **Key indicator domains:** Ideological, Foreign Contacts (sustained but disguised), Lifestyle (extended-window double life)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Long-window compromises rely on ideology more than the framework historically credited
  - The lifestyle-domain "double life" pattern was visible to a few close associates — peer interviews would have surfaced it
  - The case underscored that the "ideological" domain is not just statements; it is sustained alignment expressed through behavior

### Edward Snowden (NSA contractor, disclosure event 2013)
- **Predominant motive:** Ideology + Ego (disputed in the public record; this is the working analytic frame, not legal characterization)
- **Key indicator domains:** Technical/Access (extensive pre-disclosure download), Ideological (statements predating)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - The unilateral-discloser pattern does not match the classical foreign-recruitment shape
  - The technical/access domain (download volumes inconsistent with role, USB activity) was the key diagnostic
  - The framework adjustment: motive analysis must handle ideologically-motivated unilateral disclosure as a category, distinct from foreign-recruitment

### Reality Winner (NSA contractor, disclosure event 2017)
- **Predominant motive:** Ideology (working analytic frame)
- **Key indicator domains:** Ideological (online and personal statements), Technical/Access (printed compartmented document, leaked to media)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Ideological indicator pattern was visible on open social media before access was granted
  - Insider-threat programs began to integrate publicly-available open-source review into the indicator base after this case
  - The case is also a reminder that ideological motive can produce disclosure within months of access being granted, not only after long tenures

### Glenn Duffie Shriver (recruited as a student, China)
- **Predominant motive:** Money (initial), with developing Compromise / Commitment & Consistency
- **Key indicator domains:** Foreign Contacts (cultivated), Financial (acute), RASCLS pattern (clear)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Cultivation can begin pre-clearance; recruitment is timed to coincide with subject obtaining access
  - The RASCLS pattern (Reciprocation through paid essays, Authority through academic and professional contacts, Commitment through small disclosures) was textbook
  - The case is a strong example for `/recruit-vector-assessment` cultivation patterns

### Jerry Chun Shing Lee (former CIA officer, China)
- **Predominant motive:** Money + Ego mix
- **Key indicator domains:** Financial, Foreign Contacts (sustained, post-departure), Technical/Access (retained materials)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - Post-departure motive analysis is a legitimate frame — former cleared personnel remain CI-relevant
  - The financial indicator was acute at recruitment; the cultivation pattern visible in retrospect
  - The case underscored continuous-evaluation programs for former cleared personnel

### Insider Disclosure Cases (mid-2020s)
The mid-2020s saw several cases of cleared personnel using social platforms to publish or share classified material with non-cleared associates, most prominently in defense and intelligence-community contexts. Without identifying specific subjects beyond what the public record establishes, the pattern lessons are:
- **Predominant motives:** Ego (recognition through online communities), with occasional Ideology
- **Key indicator domains:** Technical/Access (download/print patterns), Lifestyle (online-community immersion), Ideological (less frequent)
- **Pattern lessons:**
  - The Ego-recognition motive is more empirically common than the framework historically credited
  - Online-community lifestyle indicator (extensive immersion in a community whose status hierarchy rewards leaked content) is a 21st-century domain item the indicator taxonomy must include
  - The aggregation rule applies — these cases are visible in retrospect through cross-domain pattern, not single indicators

## Pattern-Lesson Summary

Across these cases, the recurring methodological lessons are:

1. **Aggregation across domains**, not depth in any one, is what reveals motive. Single-domain analysis missed multiple of the cases above.
2. **Periodic re-examination** of cleared personnel against the full checklist catches motive drift and long-running compromises.
3. **The null hypothesis is real**, but in true-positive cases the populated checklist clearly favors a hostile motive. When the analyst has a populated checklist with consistent cross-domain corroboration, the dominant hypothesis is usually robust under ACH.
4. **The recruit-vector frame applies in some cases (Walker, Shriver) and not others (Hanssen, Snowden, Winner)**. The framework must handle both volunteer/unilateral and cultivated cases.
5. **Halo effect** repeatedly delays detection (Montes, others). Reputation is not analytic protection; the standardized checklist is.
6. **Civil-liberties protection has not been compromised** by the systematic application of standardized inspection. The cases above were caught (or are recognized in retrospect) by indicator patterns, not by demographic profiling.
7. **Ideological motive is more present in the contemporary record** than the classical Money-dominant frame predicted. The R (Revenge) and Ego categories likewise appear more often in unilateral-disclosure cases than in classical recruitment cases. The framework's MICE-RC extension is not academic — it reflects empirical patterns the analyst will encounter.

## Use of This Reference in `/build-motive-profile`

When mapping current indicators to MICE-RC categories, consult this reference to calibrate:
- Does the current pattern resemble any historical pattern? In what ways, and in what ways is it different?
- What domain coverage did the historical case have? Is the current case better- or worse-supported?
- What was the historical case's residual inconsistency? Does the current case face the same challenge?

Do not import a historical pattern as a template. Use it as a calibration anchor.
