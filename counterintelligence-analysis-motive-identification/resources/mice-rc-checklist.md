# MICE-RC Inspection Checklist — Canonical Instrument

> The standardized inspection checklist this workspace is built around. Use as a reference; the `/build-motive-profile` command operationalizes it.

## Framework Summary

MICE-RC extends the classical MICE motive framework with two additions warranted by the empirical record (PERSEREC database, public case histories):

| Letter | Motive | Brief |
|---|---|---|
| **M** | Money | Acute or chronic financial pressure or perceived enrichment opportunity |
| **I** | Ideology | Alignment with hostile interest or grievance with own state |
| **C** | Compromise | Susceptibility to blackmail; pre-existing exploitable behavior |
| **E** | Ego / Excitement | Need for recognition; thrill-seeking; resentment of perceived under-utilization |
| **R** | Revenge | Specific, documentable grievance against person, unit, or institution |
| **C** | Coercion | Active duress applied by hostile service to an unwilling subject |

## Per-Category Inspection Items

### M — Money

Use this section when the populated indicator checklist contains corroborated indicators in the Financial domain. Look for:

- [ ] Sudden unexplained affluence (new vehicle, property, luxury goods, frequent travel) inconsistent with reported income and known financial baseline
- [ ] Sudden unexplained distress (default, garnishment, multi-domain debt, collections activity)
- [ ] Foreign bank accounts not reported on SF-86 / OF-306 / equivalent
- [ ] Cash transactions just under reporting thresholds (structuring patterns)
- [ ] Gambling losses consistent with addictive patterns
- [ ] Substance-use or addiction expenses
- [ ] Family medical or eldercare costs producing acute pressure
- [ ] Investment losses concentrated in time
- [ ] Unexplained income (deposits without source documentation, anomalous tax filings)

**Aggregation rule:** Money is the most-cited motive in the public CI record but also has the highest false-positive rate. Required corroboration: financial-domain indicators **plus** at least one timestamped life-event correlate (divorce, death, business failure, major medical event) **plus** at least one secondary-domain indicator (lifestyle, technical/access).

**False-positive sources:** inheritance, divorce settlement, legitimate side business income, family medical event, late-career compensation jumps. Always rule these in or out before scoring as a Money indicator.

### I — Ideology

- [ ] Statements expressing alignment with a hostile state's stated grievance
- [ ] Active and durable hostility toward the employer's mission (not opinion, but durable action-oriented hostility)
- [ ] Affiliation with extremist organizations whose ideology endorses targeted action against the employer's mission
- [ ] Statements normalizing leak/disclosure as ethical
- [ ] Pattern of seeking compartmented information beyond role with ideological framing
- [ ] Identification with foreign cause as primary identity marker (where corroborated; not inferred from heritage)
- [ ] Romanticization of historical figures associated with the relevant ideology

**Aggregation rule:** Ideological indicators are sensitive — the bar is documentable hostility expressed in actionable form, not opinion. Required: at least two contemporaneous, attributable statements **and** at least one behavioral indicator consistent with the ideology (foreign-contact, technical/access, or lifestyle pattern).

**Critical exclusion:** Religion, ethnicity, national origin, and protected political opinion are **not** indicators absent independent corroborating behavior. Ordinary political disagreement, criticism of policy, and First-Amendment-protected expression are not indicators. Whistleblower-protected disclosures are not indicators.

### C — Compromise

- [ ] Pre-existing exploitable behavior (undisclosed affair, undisclosed gambling, undisclosed substance use, undisclosed criminal act)
- [ ] Family resident in a country with documented coercive practice toward the diaspora
- [ ] Susceptibility to blackmail revealed by polygraph or supervisor referral
- [ ] Recent security violation that has not been reported by the subject
- [ ] Behavioral pattern of risk-taking that creates exploitation opportunities
- [ ] Acquaintance compromise — known association with someone who has been compromised
- [ ] Unreported incident from earlier in the subject's career that could be leveraged

**Aggregation rule:** Compromise is *susceptibility*, not *exploitation*. A subject can have compromise indicators and not be compromised. Score Compromise when indicator pattern shows the subject is *exploitable*; this is distinct from active coercion. Compromise + Foreign-Contact in two independent sources is the canonical pattern.

### E — Ego / Excitement

- [ ] Documentable resentment of perceived under-utilization, under-promotion, or under-recognition
- [ ] Pattern of seeking access beyond role for prestige reasons
- [ ] Self-narrative as misunderstood or under-appreciated specialist
- [ ] Thrill-seeking behavior in personal or professional life
- [ ] Compulsive collection / hoarding of compartmented information beyond role
- [ ] Attention-seeking through unauthorized disclosure (often pre-disclosure boasting)
- [ ] Pattern of grandiose self-presentation inconsistent with role

**Aggregation rule:** Ego is rarely a primary motive in isolation. Most ego-driven cases are Money + Ego or Ideology + Ego. Score Ego when the indicator base includes resentment-of-recognition combined with at least one other domain. Pure Ego cases are unusual.

### R — Revenge

- [ ] Recent corrective action against the subject (discipline, demotion, denial of clearance, legal action)
- [ ] Documentable grievance with named target — supervisor, unit, agency
- [ ] Pattern of escalating workplace conflict or grievance filings
- [ ] Statements indicating intent to retaliate
- [ ] Recent loss of expected promotion or benefit, attributed to specific action by employer
- [ ] Departure or threat-of-departure pattern with hostile framing

**Aggregation rule:** Revenge is empirically a strong correlate of insider compromise (particularly disclosure-type compromise rather than recruitment-type). Required corroboration: documentable grievance **plus** access pattern indicators **plus** temporal correlation between grievance and indicator pattern. The timeline correlate is critical for Revenge.

### C — Coercion

- [ ] Direct evidence of duress (threat to family, sextortion, kidnapping)
- [ ] Behavioral fear pattern inconsistent with subject's baseline (anxiety, secrecy, avoidance)
- [ ] Indicators of forced contact with hostile service representatives
- [ ] Recent travel under unusual circumstances (compelled, sudden, with restricted communication)
- [ ] Subject has volunteered to handler that they are under duress

**Aggregation rule:** Coercion radically changes the operational response. If coercion indicators are corroborated, the response is **extraction**, not adjudication. Score Coercion when the indicator base shows duress markers; even single-source coercion indicators are escalated to operational leadership immediately, not retained for analytic completion.

## Cross-Cutting Aggregation Rules

A motive hypothesis at the level of "finding" requires:

1. **At least two domains** of indicator support (Financial, Foreign Contacts, Lifestyle, Ideological, Technical/Access)
2. **At least one timestamped indicator** to enable causation analysis
3. **A documented source** for every indicator (no anonymous-tip-only findings)
4. **Survival under ACH** against the null hypothesis and at least one alternative motive
5. **Civil-liberties review** confirming no indicator rests on protected-class characteristics absent independent corroborating behavior

A hypothesis built from one domain is **"indicator pattern of concern"** — meaningful for further inquiry, not a motive finding.

## Confidence Calibration

Use ICD 203 lexicon (probability) and separate confidence rating (high/moderate/low):

- **High confidence + high likelihood:** A-1 sources across domains, multiple timestamped corroborations, ACH dominant by margin, no civil-liberties concerns. Rare.
- **Moderate confidence + likely:** B-2/B-3 sources, single-domain timestamping, ACH dominant but with residual inconsistencies. Common.
- **Low confidence + roughly even:** F-rated sources, cross-domain gaps, ACH cannot discriminate. Common as initial finding; warrants more inquiry, not adjudication.

A "high likelihood + low confidence" finding is a real combination — it means *if* sources are accurate, the motive is highly likely, but source quality cannot be independently verified. Disclose this combination clearly.
