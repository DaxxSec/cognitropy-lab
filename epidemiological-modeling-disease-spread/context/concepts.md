# Epidemiological Disease-Spread Modeling — Core Concepts

Background the agent reads before acting on tasks. Optimised for fast recall, not exhaustive theory. The unifying view: a surveillance stream is a **time series** generated by a transmission process, observed through a delayed, noisy reporting filter. Trend analysis is the art of inverting that filter well enough to read the process.

## The surveillance pyramid (what the series actually measures)

Reported counts are the tip of a pyramid. Each layer attenuates and delays the one below:

```
infections  →  symptomatic  →  seek care  →  tested  →  test positive  →  reported  →  (revised)
```

- **Ascertainment** = reported / true infections. It is rarely stable: testing policy, care-seeking, and lab capacity all move it. A rising case count with rising test volume may be flat incidence.
- **Streams differ in lag and bias.** Cases lead but are most ascertainment-sensitive; hospitalisations lag ~1-2 weeks but are ascertainment-stable; deaths lag ~2-4 weeks; **wastewater** is ascertainment-free and leads cases by days. Triangulate across streams; never trust one in isolation.

## Compartmental models

Deterministic ODE / stochastic compartment flows. The agent fits these to incidence with `/fit-compartmental`.

| Model | Compartments | Use when |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **SIR** | Susceptible→Infectious→Recovered | No meaningful latent period; fast screening |
| **SEIR** | adds Exposed (latent, not yet infectious) | Most respiratory/viral; latent ≠ incubation |
| **SEIRS** | R→S waning immunity | Endemic pathogens, reinfection |
| **SIRD** | splits Recovered/Dead | When fitting to deaths |
| **Metapopulation** | coupled patches | Spatial spread (out of scope here; flag for hand-off) |

Key relations: **R0 = β/γ** for SIR (β = transmission rate, γ = recovery rate = 1/infectious period). Epidemic grows while **S/N > 1/R0**; the **herd-immunity threshold** is `1 − 1/R0`. Final size obeys the implicit relation `R∞ = 1 − exp(−R0·R∞)`.

**Latent vs incubation period:** latent = infection→infectiousness (drives SEIR's E→I). Incubation = infection→symptoms (drives onset-based observation). They are different and frequently confused.

## The renewal equation (the time-series backbone)

The semi-mechanistic alternative to ODEs, and the basis of most modern Rt estimation:

```
E[I_t] = R_t · Σ_{s=1}^{t} I_{t−s} · w_s
```

Expected incidence today = current reproduction number × past incidence weighted by the **generation-interval distribution** `w`. This turns Rt estimation into a regression of today's cases on a serial-interval-weighted sum of recent cases — no compartments required.

## Reproduction numbers

- **R0** — basic reproduction number: expected secondary cases from one case in a fully susceptible population. A property of pathogen × population × behaviour, *not* a pure constant.
- **Rt (effective / time-varying)** — the live number under current immunity and interventions. **Rt > 1** epidemic growing; **= 1** plateau; **< 1** shrinking. This is the headline trend metric.
  - **Instantaneous Rt (Cori/EpiEstim):** secondary cases that *current* infections will cause, given recent incidence. Estimable in near-real-time; what `/estimate-rt` produces by default.
  - **Case/cohort Rt (Wallinga-Teunis):** secondary cases *caused by* cases infected at time t. Retrospective, right-truncated, needs future data.

## Serial interval vs generation interval

- **Generation interval** — infection→infection time. The theoretically correct kernel for the renewal equation, but unobservable directly.
- **Serial interval** — symptom-onset→symptom-onset between infector/infectee. Observable; used as a proxy for the generation interval. Can be **negative** with pre-symptomatic transmission (e.g. SARS-CoV-2).
- Misspecifying the interval biases Rt: a too-short mean pulls Rt toward 1 (compresses); a too-long mean exaggerates deviations. Always state the distribution (mean, SD, parametric form) used.

## Growth rate and doubling time

In the exponential phase, `I_t ≈ I_0 · e^{r·t}`. The **growth rate r** (per day) gives **doubling time = ln(2)/r** (or halving time when r<0). r connects to Rt through the generation-interval distribution g via the **Euler-Lotka equation**: `1/R = ∫ e^{−r·a} g(a) da`. For a gamma-distributed generation interval (mean μ, shape k): `R = (1 + r·μ/k)^k`. `/growth-rate` uses this mapping.

## Reporting delays and right-truncation

The single biggest source of false trends.

- **Reporting delay** — onset/event → entry in the dataset. Often a few days to weeks, right-skewed (negative-binomial / gamma / discrete distribution).
- **Right-truncation** — recent events haven't been reported *yet*, so the latest dates are systematically undercounted. The curve always looks like it's falling at the edge. **Never read a downturn off the truncated tail.** Nowcasting (`/nowcast`) reconstructs the eventual counts from the delay distribution.
- **Backcasting / deconvolution** — shifting an observed series (e.g. reports, deaths) back to the infection dates that generated it, by deconvolving the delay distribution. Needed before mechanistic fitting.

## Time-series structure in surveillance data

- **Day-of-week effect** — strong weekly periodicity from reporting cadence (weekend dips, Monday catch-up). Strip it with a 7-day trailing mean, STL with period 7, or an explicit weekday term — never interpret it.
- **Holiday effects** — public holidays produce sharp artifactual dips + rebounds; flag and adjust.
- **Seasonality** — genuine annual periodicity (influenza, RSV winter peaks). Separate from secular trend with STL (`/decompose-trend`).
- **Overdispersion** — counts are not Poisson; variance > mean. Use negative-binomial likelihoods in detection and fitting.
- **Endemic-vs-epidemic split** — the Held–Höhle `hhh4` framework decomposes counts into an endemic baseline + autoregressive (epidemic) component; basis for principled aberration detection.

## Forecasting and its evaluation

- **Horizons:** 1-4 weeks ahead is the credible window for incidence; mechanistic scenario projections go further but are scenarios, not forecasts.
- **Methods:** renewal/Rt-projection (EpiNow2, `projections`), statistical (ARIMA, ETS, Prophet), mechanistic (fitted SEIR), and **ensembles** (combining models almost always beats any single one — the central finding of the COVID/Flu forecast hubs).
- **Probabilistic, not point.** Forecasts are predictive distributions / quantiles.
- **Scoring (`/score-forecast`):**
  - **WIS** (Weighted Interval Score) — proper score over quantile forecasts; the forecast-hub standard. Lower is better; decomposes into dispersion + over- + under-prediction.
  - **CRPS** — continuous analogue of WIS for full predictive distributions.
  - **PIT / coverage** — are the 50%/95% intervals actually covering 50%/95% of outcomes? Calibration check.
  - **Pinball/quantile loss** — per-quantile component.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Reading the right-truncated tail** — declaring a peak/decline off incomplete recent data. The cardinal sin. Nowcast first.
- **Smoothing then estimating Rt** — pre-smoothing the series before EpiEstim biases the interval and distorts Rt timing (Gostic et al.). Let the method handle noise.
- **Wrong observation date** — estimating Rt on *report* date instead of *onset/infection* date shifts Rt in time by the reporting delay.
- **Misspecified serial interval** — copying an interval from another pathogen/variant; biases every Rt.
- **Ascertainment drift read as incidence** — a testing-policy change interpreted as a real wave.
- **Poisson when overdispersed** — over-narrow intervals, false aberration alarms.
- **Point forecasts** — no interval = no honesty. Always propagate uncertainty.
- **Extrapolating exponential growth** — r is only constant early; susceptible depletion and behaviour bend the curve.

## Operating Constraints

- Case-level line lists are PHI/PII — aggregate, suppress small cells, honour HIPAA/GDPR and data-use agreements, never persist identifiers.
- Outputs are decision support with stated uncertainty, not clinical or policy directives.
- Every estimate must travel with its **data snapshot date**, the **serial/generation interval** used, and the **method + version** — surveillance data revise, so a result is only reproducible against its vintage.
